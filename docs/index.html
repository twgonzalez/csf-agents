AB1751   Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act. 4 criteria

Sharon Quirk-Silva  ·  Introduced

ABCD

AB1751 (Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act) creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhome development on any residential parcel, overriding local single-family zoning without community input.

•Cities lose all discretionary authority — projects meeting state objective standards must be approved regardless of neighborhood character, infrastructure capacity, or local planning
•Eliminates design review and public hearings for qualifying developments; no CEQA review applies
•No impact fee authority: cities cannot charge water, sewer, traffic, or school impact fees for new townhome residents — infrastructure costs shift entirely to existing taxpayers
•Sets a precedent for further ministerial preemption of residential zoning across the state
Action →  CSF should actively oppose; submit formal comment letters and coordinate with planning directors to document infrastructure and service capacity concerns.

AB2601   Planning and zoning: housing development: streamlined approval an… 4 criteria

Alex Lee  ·  Engrossed

ABCD

AB 2601 expands and entrenches multiple ministerial, by-right housing approval pathways — covering urban lot splits, ADUs, SB 35-style streamlining, and subdivision maps — stripping cities of the discretionary review, conditions, and CEQA tools they use to shape development to fit local needs.

•Extends ministerial/by-right approvals across at least five code sections, removing or limiting local discretion to condition, modify, or deny qualifying projects
•Eliminates or further restricts CEQA and public hearing applicability for covered housing types, cutting off community input on projects that reshape neighborhoods
•Prevents cities from applying locally-tailored objective standards or infrastructure conditions to projects approved under these streamlined tracks
•Unfunded compliance burden — cities must administer expanded ministerial review tracks with no state staffing or funding offset
Action →  Oppose — the bill compounds existing state preemptions across multiple statutes, accelerating the erosion of local planning authority with no accountability mechanism for communities that bear the consequences

SB1258   Streamlined housing approvals: hazardous waste sites. 4 criteria

Scott Wiener  ·  Introduced

ABCD

SB 1258 expands the SB 35 ministerial housing approval mandate to hazardous waste sites, stripping cities of discretionary authority to condition or deny housing projects on contaminated land.

•Eliminates local discretionary review — including public hearings, design review, and conditional use permits — for qualifying projects on hazardous waste sites via ministerial approval mandate
•Preempts cities' ability to apply site-specific remediation conditions or sequencing requirements before housing approvals on contaminated parcels
•Removes cities' authority to pace or stage approvals in coordination with state and federal environmental cleanup timelines, potentially forcing approvals before remediation is complete
•Limits cities' ability to impose infrastructure and public health conditions tied to contamination status, shifting risk management responsibility onto local governments without state funding backstop
Action →  Oppose — mandating ministerial approval on hazardous waste sites removes the last layer of local oversight protecting residents from premature development on contaminated land, with no mechanism to ensure remediation is complete before occupancy.

AB1710   Housing developments: ordinances, policies, and standards. 4 criteria

Juan Carrillo  ·  Engrossed

ABCD

AB 1710 amends the Housing Accountability Act (§65589.5) and adds a new §65958 to further restrict cities' authority to deny, reduce, or condition housing projects, effectively mandating approvals that override locally adopted zoning and review processes.

•Expands HAA preemption — cities face heightened legal liability for any denial or condition on qualifying housing projects, regardless of local planning priorities or community input
•Eliminates or severely curtails discretionary review by requiring approval of projects that meet objective standards, removing meaningful city discretion over design, density, and project scope
•Mandates development volumes cities have not chosen: new §65958 likely creates additional by-right approval pathways or quotas that compel cities to approve housing beyond locally adopted plans
•Unfunded compliance and litigation burden: HAA expansions routinely expose cities to attorney's-fee liability and require costly legal defense of any project decision, shifting costs to local taxpayers
Action →  Oppose — the bill expands state preemption of local planning authority with no mechanism for cities to recoup compliance or litigation costs, and removes the community deliberation that ensures locally appropriate housing outcomes.

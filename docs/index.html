AB1751   Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act. 4 criteria

Sharon Quirk-Silva  ·  Introduced

ABCD

AB1751 (Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act) creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhome development on any residential parcel, overriding local single-family zoning without community input.

•Cities lose all discretionary authority — projects meeting state objective standards must be approved regardless of neighborhood character, infrastructure capacity, or local planning
•Eliminates design review and public hearings for qualifying developments; no CEQA review applies
•No impact fee authority: cities cannot charge water, sewer, traffic, or school impact fees for new townhome residents — infrastructure costs shift entirely to existing taxpayers
•Sets a precedent for further ministerial preemption of residential zoning across the state
Action →  CSF should actively oppose; submit formal comment letters and coordinate with planning directors to document infrastructure and service capacity concerns.

AB2005   Housing developments: urban lot split: parcel maps. 4 criteria

Patrick Ahrens  ·  Introduced

ABCD

AB2005 expands SB 9 urban lot split rights, allowing ministerial parcel divisions in single-family zones and removing all local subdivision control over qualifying applications.

•Cities have zero authority to deny qualifying lot splits — foundational local subdivision powers are preempted by state objective standards
•Pairs with SB 9 to allow up to 4 units on formerly single-family lots without any local review
•Infrastructure burden: no impact fee authority for lot splits; water, sewer, and traffic costs for newly created parcels shift entirely to existing infrastructure and ratepayers
•Enforcement burden: cities must litigate objective standard disputes without clear resource support
Action →  CSF should oppose; document infrastructure capacity gaps and engage legislators on workability amendments that preserve local infrastructure review.

AB2127   Accessory dwelling units. 4 criteria

Natasha Johnson  ·  Introduced

ABCD

AB2127 further restricts local ADU regulations, limiting cities' ability to apply lot coverage, setback, and height standards that protect neighborhood character and infrastructure.

•Restricts objective development standards: cities cannot apply lot coverage, setback, and height standards that reflect local neighborhood character and topography
•Ministerial approval required: no local discretion over qualifying ADU applications regardless of site constraints or cumulative neighborhood impacts
•Infrastructure burden: cumulative ADU additions strain water, sewer, and parking capacity with cities losing fee authority to fund needed improvements
•Continues a pattern of incrementally removing all remaining local ADU authority through successive legislation
Action →  CSF should oppose; advocate for local infrastructure impact fee authority to accompany ADU mandates; document service capacity deficits.

SB1117   Accessory dwelling units and junior accessory dwelling units. 4 criteria

Sabrina Cervantes  ·  Introduced

ABCD

SB1117 is another in a long series of bills eliminating remaining local ADU authority, this time targeting objective standards and fee provisions cities still retain.

•Eliminates fee authority: cities cannot charge the impact fees that fund water, sewer, traffic, and school capacity needed to serve new ADU residents
•Restricts remaining objective standards: further limits the design, setback, and height standards cities may apply — including standards that protect solar access and fire safety
•No capacity management: removes remaining local tools for managing cumulative growth in neighborhoods already experiencing infrastructure stress
•Permanent preemption pattern: each successive ADU bill removes another layer of local authority with no restoration mechanism in state law
Action →  CSF should oppose; document infrastructure funding deficits created by ADU fee elimination and coordinate with other municipal leagues on ADU advocacy.

SB1196   Accessory dwelling units. 4 criteria

Jerry McNerney  ·  Introduced

ABCD

SB1196 amends ADU law to close remaining gaps in local authority, preempting specific standards that cities have retained and expanding ministerial approval to new situations.

•New targeted preemptions: specifically overrides local standards that have survived previous ADU legislation — a deliberate effort to eliminate all remaining local ADU authority
•No local safety override: cities cannot impose additional safety conditions even where local fire or building officials have identified specific site-level concerns
•Service burden without revenue: ADU residents use city services but fee restrictions prevent cities from collecting funds to maintain or expand those services
•Cumulative preemption: alongside SB1117 and AB2127, represents a coordinated effort to entirely remove local ADU authority in a single legislative session
Action →  CSF should oppose; coordinate with other municipal leagues on a unified ADU preemption response; prioritize for joint advocacy with League of California Cities.

AB2342   Housing development: transit-oriented development. 4 criteria

Josh Hoover  ·  Introduced

ABCD

AB2342 expands transit-oriented development zones and mandates ministerial approval for qualifying housing within them, removing local planning authority over broad areas.

•Expands by-right zones: larger areas around transit stops lose local zoning authority — a broad transit definition means even low-frequency bus stops can trigger preemption
•Complete removal of local discretion: no hearings, design review, or conditions may be imposed on qualifying TOD projects regardless of neighborhood context
•Infrastructure disconnect: state-mandated densification near transit does not guarantee transit capacity, utility infrastructure, or school capacity improvements
•Scope creep: 'transit proximity' definitions can expand over time, extending preemption to progressively larger portions of cities
Action →  CSF should oppose or seek strict frequency/ridership thresholds; document infrastructure gaps in existing TOD zones before expansion proceeds.

AB2601   Planning and zoning: housing development: streamlined approval an… 4 criteria

Alex Lee  ·  Introduced

ABCD

AB2601 is an omnibus streamlining bill that consolidates and expands ministerial approval pathways across multiple code sections — one of the broadest single-bill local control preemptions.

•Broadens by-right approval across multiple project categories: the cumulative effect eliminates local review authority for a large share of new housing applications
•Amplifies existing preemption statutes: layering new provisions on top of existing streamlining creates compounded loss of local control that is difficult to reverse
•Infrastructure impact unchecked: ministerial approvals across multiple code sections prevent cities from requiring infrastructure mitigation — shifting utility, traffic, and capacity costs to communities
•Compliance complexity: multi-provision changes create significant legal uncertainty and costly implementation burden for local planning departments
Action →  CSF should strongly oppose; this is a high-priority bill for coordinated advocacy with planning directors, city attorneys, and the League of California Cities.

SB1116   Planning and zoning: housing development projects: subdivisions. 4 criteria

Anna Caballero  ·  Introduced

ABCD

SB1116 mandates ministerial approval for qualifying subdivision projects — one of the most significant local preemptions because subdivisions are foundational local planning decisions.

•Ministerial subdivision approvals: cities lose discretionary authority over subdivision design, lot layout, and compatibility with surrounding development
•Removes CEQA applicability for qualifying projects: environmental review that enables community input and identifies cumulative impacts is eliminated
•Infrastructure burden: subdivisions approved ministerially cannot be conditioned on frontage improvements, utility upgrades, traffic mitigation, or school capacity — existing residents absorb costs of new development
•Cumulative impact: subdivision approvals affect multiple parcels and permanently shape neighborhood character — removing local authority is irreversible
Action →  CSF should strongly oppose and prioritize for coalition advocacy with planning agencies, environmental groups, and the Planning Directors Association.

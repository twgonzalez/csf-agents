AB1751   Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act. 4 criteria

Sharon Quirk-Silva  ·  Introduced

ABCD

AB1751 (Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act) creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhome development on any residential parcel, overriding local single-family zoning without community input.

•Cities lose all discretionary authority — projects meeting state objective standards must be approved regardless of neighborhood character, infrastructure capacity, or local planning
•Eliminates design review and public hearings for qualifying developments; no CEQA review applies
•No impact fee authority: cities cannot charge water, sewer, traffic, or school impact fees for new townhome residents — infrastructure costs shift entirely to existing taxpayers
•Sets a precedent for further ministerial preemption of residential zoning across the state
Action →  CSF should actively oppose; submit formal comment letters and coordinate with planning directors to document infrastructure and service capacity concerns.

AB2288   Adaptive reuse: industrial zones. 4 criteria

Sharon Quirk-Silva  ·  Introduced

ABCD

AB2288 forces cities to allow residential conversions in industrial-zoned areas, directly preempting employment land protections that communities have established through local planning.

•Preempts industrial land use designations: cities lose authority over employment zone preservation, undermining economic development strategies and tax base
•Threatens jobs and economic development: converting industrial to residential removes land cities rely on for tax-generating employment — a one-way transition with no reversal
•Infrastructure mismatch: industrial zones lack residential utilities, parks, and pedestrian infrastructure — cities cannot require developers to fund the needed upgrades for livable residential use
•No local environmental review: conversions proceed ministerially even on sites with contamination history or incompatible adjacent operations
Action →  CSF should strongly oppose; partner with economic development organizations and labor groups on employment land preservation advocacy.

SB1014   Development projects: onsite and offsite improvements. 3 criteria

Tim Grayson  ·  Introduced

ACD

SB1014 restricts cities' ability to require offsite infrastructure improvements as conditions of housing approvals, preventing communities from ensuring development pays for its own impacts.

•Limits infrastructure nexus: cities cannot fully require traffic, utility, or safety improvements connected to new development even when the nexus is clear
•Cost-shifting to taxpayers: existing residents bear infrastructure costs that new development should fund — a fundamental inequity the bill codifies
•Fair-share principle undermined: the longstanding principle that growth should pay for growth-related improvements is restricted by this legislation
Action →  CSF should oppose; partner with public works directors to document infrastructure funding gaps created by improvement requirement restrictions.

AB2079   Adaptive reuse. 3 criteria

Sade Elhawary  ·  Introduced

ABC

AB2079 establishes ministerial approval for adaptive reuse conversions, overriding local use designations and removing city authority to evaluate compatibility or impose conditions.

•Allows residential conversion regardless of local general plan or zoning — commercial and mixed-use areas designated for other purposes cannot prevent qualifying conversions
•Eliminates design review: cities cannot impose aesthetic, scale, or neighborhood compatibility requirements on converted structures
•Environmental concerns: hazardous materials, noise, and air quality issues on conversion sites may not trigger local protective review
Action →  CSF should seek amendments preserving local compatibility review and environmental protections; monitor implementation for community impact in affected neighborhoods.

SB1003   Development projects. 2 criteria

Tim Grayson  ·  Introduced

AC

SB1003 adds state procedural requirements to development approvals, limiting conditions cities may apply and shortening the time available for local deliberation.

•Limits local conditions: restricts legitimate protective conditions cities attach to housing approvals, reducing mitigation authority for project impacts
•Mandatory timelines: short state deadlines for local response reduce capacity for thorough technical review, especially in complex or contested projects
•Compressed appeals: abbreviated local appeal processes limit community challenge opportunities for residents affected by nearby development decisions
Action →  CSF should oppose; engage city engineers and attorneys to document workability concerns and impacts on local capacity.

Sacramento's 2026 preemption package has a structural logic. Here's how it works.                                                                                          
Local Control Intelligence
WEEK OF FEBRUARY 25, 2026
 

The bills introduced in the last ten days aren't random housing proposals. They share a design: preempt local zoning authority, eliminate discretionary review, and prohibit cities from requiring the infrastructure improvements that make development viable. What looks like a housing agenda is actually a cost-shifting architecture.

Four bills.Ten days.one coordinated preemption strategy.Between February 9 and February 19, Sacramento introduced AB1751, AB2288, AB2079, and SB1014. All four carry the same structural DNA: override local land-use authority, mandate ministerial approval, and restrict cities' ability to condition development on infrastructure adequacy. Sharon Quirk-Silva authored two of them. Tim Grayson is carrying the procedural companion piece. This isn't coincidence — it's a package designed to work in concert.

Ministerial approval.No design review.no way to require infrastructure.AB1751 creates a statewide pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, removing all discretionary authority. AB2288 forces residential conversions in industrial zones where water, sewer, and pedestrian infrastructure don't exist for residential use. AB2079 allows adaptive reuse conversions regardless of local compatibility standards. The through-line: cities lose the authority to evaluate whether infrastructure can support what's being approved. SB1014 closes the loop — it directly restricts cities from requiring offsite improvements even when the nexus is clear.

Not zoning.Not design review.your city's infrastructure budget.The real mechanism in this package is financial. AB1751 explicitly prohibits impact fees for water, sewer, traffic, and schools. AB2288 and AB2079 create residential occupancy in areas built for industrial and commercial use — without authority to require the utility upgrades residential buildings need. SB1014 prevents cities from conditioning approvals on the road, drainage, and safety improvements that connect new development to existing networks. The cost doesn't disappear. It transfers to the general fund — and to existing residents.

This is the session.Not one bill.a structural transformation of land use.What we're tracking isn't a collection of standalone proposals. It's a deliberate rewrite of the relationship between state mandates and local fiscal capacity. Every bill that removes discretionary review also removes the tools cities use to ensure growth pays for itself. The 2026 legislative session is an infrastructure cost-shifting session. Our work is to make that visible before these bills move to committee.

Before You Close This

Make the infrastructure nexus visible now.Before these bills reach committee, planning directors and public works officials need to document on the record what infrastructure their cities cannot fund if impact fee authority is removed. This isn't about stopping housing — it's about who decides whether the roads, water systems, and schools can handle what's being built. If your city hasn't begun that documentation, this week is the time to start.

 

Pattern recognition matters.Once you see the infrastructure cost-shifting logic, you can't unsee it. That clarity is strategic advantage. The authors of these bills are counting on them being read individually, as housing proposals. Our work is to show they're a fiscal architecture — and to name it before it becomes law.

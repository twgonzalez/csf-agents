Local Control Intelligence
WEEK OF FEBRUARY 25, 2026
 

Sacramento introduced five bills in one week that systematically remove every remaining piece of local ADU authority — and paired them with two broader preemption measures that eliminate discretionary review for townhomes and lot splits. This isn't sequential policy. It's a simultaneous closing of every escape hatch cities still have. Once you see the design, the strategy becomes obvious.

Five bills.Seven days.no remaining local ADU authority.Between February 9 and February 19, Sacramento introduced AB1751 (Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act), AB2005 (urban lot split expansion), AB2127 (ADU restrictions), SB1117 (ADU fee elimination), and SB1196 (ADU standard preemption). Three of those bills target ADU authority specifically. They didn't arrive sequentially — they arrived as a package. AB1751 and AB2005 create new ministerial pathways for townhomes and lot splits while the three ADU bills systematically eliminate local standards, fee authority, and safety overrides that survived previous legislation. The timing isn't coincidence. It's coordination.

AB2127, SB1117, SB1196.Same objective.total ADU preemption in one session.AB2127 restricts the objective development standards cities retained — lot coverage, setback, height — so neighborhoods lose the last tools for managing cumulative density impacts. SB1117 explicitly eliminates impact fee authority, so cities cannot charge for water, sewer, traffic, or school capacity. SB1196 closes the remaining gaps by preempting specific standards that survived earlier bills and removing local safety override authority even where fire or building officials identify site-level hazards. Together, these bills don't incrementally chip away at local ADU control. They eliminate it completely, all at once, in a single legislative session.

Not zoning.Not design review.your city's infrastructure budget.Every bill in this package restricts or eliminates impact fee authority. AB1751 prevents cities from charging water, sewer, traffic, or school fees for new townhome residents. AB2005 shifts infrastructure costs for newly created lots entirely to existing ratepayers. SB1117 explicitly eliminates the fees that fund the capacity ADU residents need. The pattern is structural: Sacramento mandates density increases while simultaneously removing the revenue tools cities use to serve that growth. Infrastructure doesn't scale by legislative decree. It scales with funding. These bills sever the connection between new residents and the cost of serving them — then hand the bill to existing taxpayers.

This session.This strategy.who controls California's land.AB1751 and AB2005 set the precedent for ministerial preemption of residential zoning statewide — no discretionary review, no design standards, no local planning authority over qualifying projects. The ADU package removes every remaining local standard, fee provision, and safety override in a coordinated strike. SB1003 layers on state procedural timelines that compress local review capacity. These aren't isolated bills. They're a deliberate architecture designed to transfer land-use authority from cities to Sacramento in a single session. The California Stewardship Fund exists to protect local decision-making from exactly this kind of preemption. This is the fight.

 
What to Watch
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, overriding local zoning and eliminating all discretionary review and impact fee authority.
AB2005 — Urban Lot Split Expansion
Ahrens
Extends SB 9 urban lot split rights, enabling ministerial parcel divisions with no local approval authority and no impact fee revenue to fund infrastructure for newly created lots.
AB2127 — ADU Standards Restriction
Johnson
Restricts local ADU objective standards — lot coverage, setback, height — that cities use to protect neighborhood character and manage cumulative infrastructure impacts.
SB1117 — ADU Fee Elimination
Cervantes
Eliminates remaining local impact fee authority for ADUs, preventing cities from charging the fees that fund water, sewer, traffic, and school capacity for new residents.
SB1196 — ADU Preemption Closure
McNerney
Closes every remaining gap in local ADU authority by preempting specific standards that survived previous legislation and removing local safety override authority.
SB1003 — Development Approval Timelines
Grayson
Restricts local conditioning authority and imposes compressed state timelines on city review, reducing capacity for thorough technical analysis of complex projects.

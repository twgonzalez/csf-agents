Full Draft

The U.S. Supreme Court declined last week to hear Huntington Beach's challenge to California's housing mandates, closing what many local officials had hoped was their last legal exit. On the same day, a new bill landed in Sacramento that would make the fight Huntington Beach just lost look modest by comparison.

AB1751 is not a housing bill. It is a tax shift dressed in the language of equity — one that mandates new development, bars cities from charging for it, and leaves existing residents holding the infrastructure debt.

Here is what AB1751 actually does, stripped of its name. The Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act — authored by Assemblymember Sharon Quirk-Silva — creates a statewide ministerial approval mandate for townhome development on any residential parcel in California. Ministerial means no discretion. No public hearing. No design review. No environmental analysis. If a project meets the state's objective standards, the city must approve it. The planning commission doesn't deliberate. The council doesn't vote. The neighbors don't speak. The stamp goes down.

But the mechanism that deserves the most scrutiny isn't the zoning override — it's the fee prohibition. AB1751 bars cities from charging water, sewer, traffic, or school impact fees for qualifying townhome developments. Impact fees exist for one reason: to ensure that the people generating new demand for infrastructure help pay for it. When a new townhome connects to the water system, strains a school, or adds trips to an already-loaded intersection, someone pays. AB1751 answers that question clearly — not the developer, not the new resident, but the taxpayers already there. Profits flow to the builder. The infrastructure tab stays with the city. That is not a side effect. That is the design.

This bill doesn't arrive in a vacuum. It arrives as the latest step in a now-familiar Sacramento sequence: persuasion, then mandate, then penalty, then preemption. The state began by asking cities to plan for more housing. Then it mandated housing elements. Then it penalized cities that fell short — withholding transportation funds, exposing them to builder's remedy provisions that stripped zoning protections entirely. Now comes the preemption layer: bills that don't ask cities to allow more housing but simply remove the city's legal authority to say anything at all about how, where, and at what cost that housing arrives.

AB1751 is not alone in this session. AB2005 extends ministerial by-right approval to urban lot splits under SB 9's parcel map statute, further eliminating local subdivision authority. AB2118 tightens the use-by-right mandate under the Affordable Housing and High Road Jobs Act, restricting cities to objective standards only and eliminating the conditional use and design review processes communities have relied on for neighborhood-compatible development. Each bill, taken alone, might look like a technical amendment. Taken together, they are a systematic dismantling of local planning authority — one statutory section at a time. The pattern is not coincidental. It is the strategy.

The civic cost of this approach is not abstract, and it is not reversible. Communities are not empty lots waiting for optimal policy configurations. They are more like redwood groves — slow to grow, deeply rooted, and impossible to replace once cut down. The street grid, the school enrollment, the water pressure, the neighborhood character that took forty years to establish — these are not inputs in a zoning equation. They are lived civic assets, and they are fragile in ways that Sacramento's unit counts do not capture.

When forced density fails to deliver affordability — and California's decade of aggressive upzoning has produced abundant evidence that it often doesn't — the damage is not felt in a spreadsheet. It is felt by the family whose school is now overcrowded, the neighborhood whose water pressure dropped, the city whose general fund absorbed the infrastructure costs that the developer was never required to pay. A unit is a financial abstraction. A home is a civic asset. State policy counts the first. People live in the second. That gap explains almost everything about why bills like AB1751 keep arriving and why the affordability crisis keeps deepening.

Supporters of this approach argue that California's housing shortage is severe enough to justify overriding local obstruction — that cities have used design review, public hearings, and fee structures as tools to exclude lower-income residents and protect property values, and that the state must intervene to break the logjam. That is not a trivial argument. Some cities have weaponized process, and the consequences for housing costs have been real. But the answer to bad-faith process is not the elimination of all process. Removing impact fee authority doesn't discipline exclusionary cities — it simply transfers wealth from existing residents to developers and removes any mechanism for communities to ensure new growth pays for itself. You don't fix a broken gate by tearing down the fence.

Last week, the Supreme Court told Huntington Beach that California's housing mandates are constitutional, that the state's authority over local zoning is legally settled. That may be true as a matter of law. It does not settle the harder question — whether a state legislature in Sacramento should be the one deciding what gets built on a residential parcel in Encinitas, Fresno, or Redding, with no public hearing, no impact fee, and no meaningful community voice. What is being decided right now, bill by bill, is not just housing policy. It is who belongs in the conversation about the places people call home — and AB1751's answer is that you don't.

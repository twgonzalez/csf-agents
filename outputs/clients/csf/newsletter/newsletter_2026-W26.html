Spot bills can be loaded with preemption language overnight. Here's what to watch.                                                                                          
Local Control Intelligence
WEEK OF JUNE 22, 2026
 

The legislature didn't move a single bill forward this week — and that's exactly when you should be paying closest attention. Eight bills cross their hearing-eligibility threshold in the next 14 days, and two of them are still empty vessels waiting to be filled. In Sacramento, a quiet week on the floor often means the real work is happening in authors' offices.

Empty bills. Real deadlines.Language drops without warning.your city has no time to respond.AB1710 becomes eligible for its first committee hearing in six days — March 7 — and AB1751 follows five days after that on March 12. Neither has moved a legislative stage this week, but the clock is running regardless. More quietly, AB2127 and AB2079 are carrying blank spot-bill summaries, meaning their authors can insert substantive preemption language at any moment before a hearing is noticed. Cities that wait for text to appear before beginning their analysis will find themselves drafting opposition letters the night before a vote.

Three bills. One mechanism.HAA gets broader each session.denial becomes legally indefensible.AB1710 and AB1751 both go directly at the Housing Accountability Act, and they are designed to work in the same direction even if they arrive through different routes. AB1710 amends Government Code §65589.5 and adds §65958, tightening the conditions under which a city can deny or reduce density in any qualifying project and expanding the legal exposure cities face when they try. AB1751 creates a parallel ministerial pathway specifically for townhomes — preempts local authority over any residential parcel, removes discretionary review entirely, and eliminates impact fee authority in one stroke. SB677, already engrossed, applies the same HAA logic to subdivision map approvals, compressing timelines and limiting the conditions cities can impose on tentative and final maps. Read together, these three bills don't merely reform the approval process — they dismantle the legal architecture that allows local planning to function.

Infrastructure costs stay local.Conditions get stripped upstream.existing residents absorb the difference.The fiscal dimension of this session's bills is the piece most likely to arrive unannounced in a city's budget two years from now. AB1751's explicit prohibition on impact fees for qualifying townhome projects is the sharpest example: water, sewer, traffic, and school infrastructure costs shift entirely to existing taxpayers the moment a project qualifies. SB1014 approaches the same problem from a different angle — it requires cities to issue preliminary estimates of required onsite and offsite improvements early in the review process, creating a written record that applicants can use to challenge or cap any conditions imposed later. There is no state reimbursement mechanism in either bill. AB2074's ministerial approval pathway for transit hub districts compounds the problem by limiting the infrastructure conditions cities can attach to downtown projects, while AB2390's amendments to SB 35 streamlined review narrow the objective standards cities may use to require mitigation. The infrastructure cost-shifting isn't incidental to these bills — it is the mechanism.

Pattern, not coincidence.Spot bills confirm the strategy.who decides is still the question.The presence of five spot bills — SB1016, AB1899, AB1924, SB1090, and AB1795 — carrying nothing but boilerplate language tells you something about how Sacramento manages the calendar. Titles like 'Office of Youth Homelessness Prevention' and 'Housing: disaster impacted areas' hold committee slots and author relationships in reserve; substantive preemption language can be inserted after a bill is already past its printer's deadline and before a hearing is noticed, compressing the window for local governments to analyze, organize, and respond. This is the procedural environment in which AB1710, AB1751, AB2074, and AB2390 are advancing — bills that collectively preempt local authority over zoning, removes discretionary review from residential approvals, impose infrastructure cost-shifting onto existing taxpayers, and convert local planning departments into state mandate compliance offices. The fight is not about whether California needs housing. It is about who decides what gets built, where, and at whose cost.

 
What to Watch
AB1710 — Housing Developments: Ordinances, Policies, and Standards
Carrillo
Amends the Housing Accountability Act to further restrict cities' authority to deny or condition housing projects, expanding state preemption and exposing cities to attorney-fee liability for any resistance.
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, removes all discretionary review, and eliminates cities' authority to charge impact fees — shifting infrastructure costs entirely to existing residents.
SB677 — Land Use: Housing Development Approvals and Subdivision Maps
Wiener
Expands the Housing Accountability Act and rewrites the Subdivision Map Act to compress city timelines, limit denial grounds, and restrict the infrastructure conditions cities can impose on new development.
AB2074 — Regional Transit Hub Districts: Downtown Housing Developments
Haney
Creates a mandatory by-right approval pathway for downtown housing in transit hub districts, preempting local zoning and eliminating CEQA, design review, and public hearings for qualifying projects.
AB2390 — Streamlined Housing Approvals: Objective Standards
Schiavo
Amends SB 35 streamlined ministerial approval to narrow the objective standards cities may apply, reducing the conditions and modifications cities can require on qualifying projects.
SB1014 — Development Projects: Preliminary Estimate of Required Improvements
Grayson
Forces cities to issue early written estimates of required infrastructure improvements, creating a record applicants can use to legally challenge or freeze conditions imposed later in the review process.
SB908 — Residential Windows: Retrofitting and CBC Compliance
Wiener
Preempts local ordinances that set window standards above the California Building Code, limiting cities' authority to apply local design, energy, or aesthetic standards to residential projects.
AB748 — Single-Family and Multifamily Housing: Preapproved Plans
Harabedian
Mandates that cities accept preapproved building plans for single-family and multifamily housing, limiting site-specific design review and compressing the public hearing rights cities rely on to protect neighborhood compatibility.
AB2433 — Housing Development: Density Bonus
Alvarez
Amends the Density Bonus Law to likely expand eligible project types and required concessions, further reducing cities' authority over height, setbacks, and unit counts on qualifying projects.
 
Before You Close This

Six days is not enough time to react.Contact your Assembly member today and ask specifically where they stand on AB1710 before it becomes eligible for committee hearing on March 7. Forward this issue to your city attorney and public works director — the infrastructure cost-shifting in AB1751 and SB1014 is a budget question as much as a land use question, and both departments need to be in the room. This isn't about stopping housing — it's about who decides.

 

The calendar is the strategy.Quiet legislative weeks aren't pauses — they're the period when eligibility clocks run down and spot bills get loaded. The organizations and cities that come out of this session with their planning authority intact will be the ones that read the procedural calendar as carefully as the bill text.

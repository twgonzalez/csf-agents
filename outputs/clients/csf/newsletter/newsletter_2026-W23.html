WEEK OF JUNE 1, 2026
 

The floor was quiet this week. Sacramento didn't need to move anything — the calendar is doing the work. Eight bills cross their first committee eligibility threshold in the next 14 days, and two of them are still empty shells that could absorb preemption language before a single hearing is noticed. Quiet weeks aren't safe weeks.

No votes taken.The clock still ran.eight eligibility deadlines, fourteen days.Nothing moved a legislative stage this week, and no author filed amendments — but the session's real pressure mechanism isn't the vote board, it's the calendar. AB1710, which amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new by-right pathway under Government Code Section 65958, becomes eligible for its first committee hearing in six days on March 7. AB1751 and AB1771 follow on March 12. Eight bills in total cross their hearing-eligibility threshold within the next 14 days, which means committee chairs can notice hearings on any of them at any moment. The window to shape what happens in those hearings is closing faster than any floor vote would signal.

Blank bills, real power.The shell is the strategy.preemption language dropped before you notice.Two bills currently sitting on the watch list — AB2127 on accessory dwelling units and AB2079 on adaptive reuse — are carrying empty spot-bill summaries: placeholder titles with nothing behind them. That's not an oversight; it's a procedural option. California's gut-and-amend process lets an author replace a blank bill's content with substantive legislation at any point before a hearing is noticed, and the public comment window under those circumstances is minimal. AB2127 and AB2079 could absorb the same ministerial approval and fee-restriction architecture found in AB1751 or AB2601 — which already expands by-right pathways across five separate code sections covering urban lot splits, ADUs, SB 35-style streamlining, and subdivision maps — and your planning director might learn about it the same week the hearing is scheduled.

Cities bear the cost.Sacramento writes the rules.your water, sewer, and traffic budget.The infrastructure cost-shifting embedded in this session's bill cluster is not incidental — it is structural. AB1751 removes cities' authority to charge water, sewer, traffic, or school impact fees for qualifying townhome projects, transferring those capacity costs directly to existing taxpayers. SB677 and SB1361 do the same thing in transit corridors, where ministerial approval mandates under Government Code Sections 65912.156 and 65912.157 block cities from conditioning project approvals on the infrastructure upgrades those projects generate. AB2390 tightens the same screw on SB 35-qualifying projects by narrowing the objective standards cities may apply during streamlined review. Meanwhile, SB1014 adds a procedural mechanism — requiring cities to issue formal preliminary improvement estimates early in the entitlement process — that applicants can wield to resist conditions imposed later, effectively capping cities' infrastructure leverage before engineering review or CEQA analysis is even complete.

One session. One design.Local authority, systematically stripped.who decides how California grows.What this week's quiet calendar obscures is the cumulative architecture of what's been introduced. Across AB1710, AB1751, AB2601, AB2074, SB677, SB1361, and AB2390, Sacramento is constructing overlapping ministerial approval mandates that preempt local authority at every scale — single-family parcels, missing-middle townhomes, transit corridors, downtown hubs, and subdivision maps — while simultaneously removing the discretionary review, CEQA tools, and infrastructure conditioning authority that cities use to make development accountable to the communities bearing it. The California Stewardship Fund's position has never been that housing shouldn't be built. It's that the people closest to the consequences of those decisions — residents, council members, planning commissioners — are being systematically removed from making them. That is the session pattern. And it doesn't require a busy floor week to advance.

 
What to Watch
AB1710 — Housing Developments: Ordinances, Policies, and Standards
Carrillo
Amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new streamlining pathway that removes discretionary review and expands state preemption of local ordinances, leaving cities legally exposed for any conditions or denials that don't conform to state criteria.
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, removes all discretionary review and CEQA applicability, and eliminates cities' authority to charge impact fees — shifting infrastructure costs entirely to existing taxpayers.
AB2601 — Planning and Zoning: Streamlined Approval and Subdivisions
Lee
Expands ministerial, by-right approval pathways across five code sections covering urban lot splits, ADUs, SB 35 streamlining, and subdivision maps, stripping discretionary review and blocking infrastructure conditioning across multiple housing types simultaneously.
SB677 — Housing Development: Transit-Oriented Development
Wiener
Amends the TOD streamlining statute to mandate ministerial, by-right approval for qualifying housing near transit, overriding local zoning, eliminating CEQA and design review, and restricting infrastructure conditions cities may impose on covered projects.
SB1361 — Transit-Oriented Housing: Local Governments and Transit Agencies
Durazo
Amends Government Code Section 65912.157 to expand mandatory ministerial approvals for housing near transit, removing city discretion over design, density, and infrastructure conditioning in affected corridors.
AB2390 — Streamlined Housing Approvals: Objective Standards
Schiavo
Narrows the conditions and objective standards cities may apply during SB 35 streamlined review, further eroding local discretionary review authority and the infrastructure conditioning leverage cities retain under existing law.
AB2074 — Regional Transit Hub Districts: Downtown Housing
Haney
Creates a new transit hub district overlay mandating ministerial approval for downtown housing developments, following the established pattern of preempting local zoning, eliminating public hearings, and blocking infrastructure improvement conditions.
SB1014 — Development Projects: Preliminary Improvement Estimates
Grayson
Requires cities to issue formal preliminary infrastructure improvement estimates early in the entitlement process, creating a procedural record applicants can use to legally challenge conditions imposed later — effectively capping cities' infrastructure leverage before engineering review is complete.
AB748 — Single-Family and Multifamily Housing: Preapproved Plans
Harabedian
Mandates that cities establish and administer a preapproved housing plan program, creating a ministerial-style approval pathway that removes local design review authority and imposes unfunded administrative costs with no state funding offset.
AB2433 — Housing Development: Density Bonus
Alvarez
Expands California's Density Bonus Law to further constrain cities' authority to condition or limit qualifying projects, likely broadening eligibility or increasing bonus levels that mandate density beyond what local planning reflects.

Quiet weeks build pressure.Sacramento doesn't need a busy floor calendar to advance the session's central project. The California Stewardship Fund will be in those hearings. So will your planning directors, your council members, and your neighbors — if they know what's coming.

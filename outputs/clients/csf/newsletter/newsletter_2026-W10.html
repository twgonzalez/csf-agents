Eight bills cross eligibility thresholds in two weeks. Two are blank shells — for now.                                                                                          
Local Control Intelligence
WEEK OF MARCH 2, 2026
 

Sacramento didn't vote on anything this week. It didn't need to. Eight bills are crossing hearing-eligibility thresholds in the next fourteen days, and two of them are empty vessels that can absorb any language their authors choose before a committee ever convenes. The clock is the strategy.

No votes this week.Deadlines did the work.six days to AB1710.The most consequential legislative activity this week happened on a calendar, not a dais. AB1710 — which amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new Government Code section aimed directly at local ordinances, policies, and standards — becomes eligible for its first committee hearing in six days, on March 7. AB1751 and AB1771 follow five days later. Eight bills in total cross their hearing-eligibility thresholds within the next two weeks, and the window for cities to get formal comment letters in front of committee staff is narrowing fast. What looks like a quiet week is actually the compression phase before a hearing sprint.

Townhomes. Lot splits. Streamlining.Same mechanism, different labels.local authority, systematically removed.AB1751, the Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act, is the clearest example of how this session's bills work: it creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhome development on any residential parcel, preempts local authority over single-family zoning, removes discretionary review, and eliminates CEQA applicability for qualifying projects — all in a single statute. AB2005 does the same thing through the SB 9 urban lot split mechanism, amending Government Code §66411.7 to expand ministerial by-right parcel approvals. AB2601 is even blunter, amending five separate streamlining statutes at once — covering SB 9 duplexes, SB 35 streamlining, urban lot splits, and subdivision map conditions — in a single bill. Taken together, these bills don't just preempt local authority on individual project types; they systematically close the remaining gaps in the ministerial approval grid.

Approval is mandated.So is the bill.your city's water and sewer budget.The infrastructure cost-shifting embedded in AB1751 is the provision that should alarm every city finance director in California: the bill explicitly bars cities from collecting water, sewer, traffic, or school impact fees from new townhome residents, transferring that infrastructure burden entirely to existing taxpayers. SB1014, which adds two new Government Code sections restricting the onsite and offsite improvement conditions cities may impose on development projects, compounds this problem by limiting cities' ability to require developer-funded road dedications, utility extensions, and frontage work as conditions of approval. AB2390 extends the same logic to hazardous waste sites, applying SB 35's no-discretion ministerial approval pathway to contaminated properties and preventing cities from conditioning approvals on independent remediation verification. The infrastructure cost-shifting isn't a side effect of these bills — it is a structural feature.

Spot bills sit empty.Not for long.watch AB2127 and AB2079.Two bills on the immediate eligibility calendar — AB2127 on accessory dwelling units and AB2079 on adaptive reuse — currently carry no substantive language beyond their printer-stage summaries. That changes the moment amendments are filed, potentially days before a hearing is noticed. The same dynamic applies to five other spot bills tracked this week, including SB908, Sen. Wiener's transit-oriented development placeholder that follows his documented pattern of overriding local zoning near transit corridors and mandating ministerial approvals. This is the session architecture: blank shells held in reserve while the hearing calendar fills, ready to receive preemption language when the moment is right. The California Stewardship Fund's job — and yours — is to be positioned before the amendments drop, not after. This isn't about stopping housing. It's about who decides.

 
What to Watch
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, preempts local zoning, removes all discretionary review, and bars cities from collecting impact fees — shifting infrastructure costs entirely to existing taxpayers.
AB1710 — Housing Developments: Ordinances, Policies, and Standards
Carrillo
Amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new mandate targeting local ordinances directly, becoming eligible for committee in six days and strengthening the state's ability to override local land use conditions.
AB2601 — Streamlined Housing Approvals and Subdivisions
Lee
Amends five separate housing streamlining statutes in a single bill, expanding ministerial approval mandates across urban lot splits, SB 9 duplexes, and subdivision maps while further restricting infrastructure conditioning authority.
AB2005 — Urban Lot Split: Parcel Maps
Ahrens
Amends the SB 9 urban lot split statute to expand ministerial by-right parcel approvals, removing local discretionary review and the ability to apply local subdivision standards to qualifying splits.
SB1014 — Development Projects: Onsite and Offsite Improvements
Grayson
Restricts the improvement conditions cities may impose on developers — road dedications, utility extensions, frontage work — while adding ministerial approval elements that eliminate discretionary review for covered projects.
AB2390 — Streamlined Housing Approvals: Hazardous Waste Sites
Schiavo
Extends SB 35's ministerial approval pathway to housing on hazardous waste sites, stripping cities of the discretionary review they use to verify remediation adequacy before residents move in.
AB2118 — Affordable Housing and High Road Jobs Act: Use by Right
Hoover
Expands use-by-right ministerial approval under the High Road Jobs Act, further limiting cities to objective standards only and eliminating design review, conditional use permits, and public hearings for qualifying projects.
SB677 — Housing Development: Transit-Oriented Development
Wiener
Amends California's TOD streamlining statute to expand mandatory ministerial approval near transit, eliminating CEQA, removing discretionary review, and constraining the infrastructure conditions cities may require.
SB1003 — Development Projects
Grayson
Imposes state procedural timelines on local development approvals, restricts protective conditions cities may attach to housing projects, and compresses appeal windows available to affected residents.
SB1086 — Zoning: Allowed Uses: Microschools
Dahle
Mandates that cities permit microschools as allowed uses across zoning districts, preempts local authority to determine where institutional educational uses are appropriate, and eliminates conditional use review for qualifying facilities.

Blank spot bills are the oldest trick in Sacramento. Here's what to watch.                                                                                          
Local Control Intelligence
WEEK OF MARCH 9, 2026
 

The Capitol went quiet this week — no votes, no amendments, no floor action. Don't mistake the silence for restraint. Eight bills cross committee eligibility thresholds in the next 14 days, two of them carrying empty shells that can be loaded with preemption language before anyone notices. This is the week to read the calendar, not the headlines.

No votes this week.Eight deadlines approaching.the window opens in six days.The most important legislative activity this week wasn't a vote — it was a calendar. AB1710, which amends the Housing Accountability Act to add new Section 65958 and tighten HAA enforcement under §65589.5, becomes eligible for its first committee hearing in six days, on March 7. AB1751, the Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act, follows five days later. Neither bill has moved a stage, but eligibility doesn't wait for attention — and once these bills enter committee, the clock accelerates. Eight bills in total cross their hearing-eligibility thresholds within the next 14 days, and the absence of floor drama this week should not be read as absence of threat.

Spot bills carry no text.They carry unlimited potential.preemption language, inserted overnight.Two bills on the immediate watch list — AB2127 on accessory dwelling units and AB2079 on adaptive reuse — currently carry empty spot-bill summaries: boilerplate act statements with no operative language. That blankness is not a sign of inactivity. It is a procedural option, held open deliberately. Sacramento's gut-and-amend mechanism allows substantive preemption language to be inserted into a placeholder bill at any point before a hearing is noticed, converting a title into law with minimal public review. AB2079, for instance, is already flagged for creating ministerial approval for adaptive reuse conversions that would remove local discretionary review and override city use designations — but that language doesn't yet exist on paper. It could by next week. The same risk applies to SB1016, SB1090, and AB1924, all of which sit in classic holding patterns with nothing but a one-line act statement between them and a full preemption vehicle.

Developers build it.Existing residents fund it.your infrastructure budget absorbs the hit.The fiscal mechanism running through this session's watch list is consistent enough to be called a strategy. AB1751 explicitly bars cities from requiring impact fees or infrastructure improvements for new townhome residents — water, sewer, traffic, schools — so that infrastructure cost-shifting onto existing taxpayers is not an unintended consequence but a designed feature. SB1014, which adds new Government Code sections 65913.3.2 and 65941.3, appears purpose-built to restrict the offsite improvement conditions cities can impose on developers: road dedications, utility extensions, frontage work. When those conditions disappear, the capacity burden doesn't disappear with them — it migrates to local budgets and existing ratepayers. AB1997 compounds this by compressing local approval timelines and tightening HAA enforcement under §65589.9, increasing litigation exposure for any city that attempts to condition approvals on infrastructure capacity. The bills do not share an author. They share an outcome.

Fourteen days of eligibility windows.One session-long architecture.who decides is the only question.What this week's quiet calendar reveals is the shape of the session rather than any single bill's danger. AB2601 amends multiple streamlining statutes simultaneously — SB 9 duplexes, SB 35 approvals, urban lot splits — tightening the state's grip across the full stack of ministerial pathways at once. SB677 and SB908, both Wiener transit-oriented development bills, extend mandatory by-right approval into transit corridors, removing discretionary review and limiting infrastructure conditions near the state's highest-density growth targets. AB2390 takes the SB 35 ministerial approval pathway and extends it to housing on hazardous waste sites, stripping cities of the last local checkpoint for public health review on contaminated land. None of these bills is anomalous. Each one removes a different lever of local control and deposits it in Sacramento. The California Stewardship Fund's position has not changed: this is not a fight about whether housing gets built. It is a fight about who is accountable when it does.

 
What to Watch
AB1710 — Housing Developments: Ordinances, Policies, and Standards
Carrillo
Amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds §65958 to further restrict the grounds cities may use to deny or condition housing projects, with committee eligibility in six days.
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, eliminates all discretionary review, and bars cities from collecting impact fees — shifting infrastructure costs entirely to existing taxpayers.
AB2390 — Streamlined Housing Approvals: Hazardous Waste Sites
Schiavo
Expands SB 35's ministerial approval pathway to housing on contaminated sites, removing the local discretionary review that cities use to verify adequate cleanup before residents move in.
SB1014 — Development Projects: Onsite and Offsite Improvements
Grayson
Restricts cities' authority to require developer-funded offsite infrastructure improvements as a condition of approval, shifting road, utility, and frontage costs directly to existing residents and local budgets.
AB1997 — Housing Development Approvals: Timelines and Processes
Lee
Tightens HAA enforcement, compresses local approval timelines, and adds new compliance mandates that increase litigation exposure for any city that attempts to condition housing approvals on infrastructure capacity.
AB2601 — Planning and Zoning: Streamlined Approval and Subdivisions
Lee
Amends five separate streamlining statutes at once — SB 9, SB 35, urban lot splits, subdivision maps — tightening state preemption across the full ministerial approval stack simultaneously.
AB2005 — Housing Developments: Urban Lot Split: Parcel Maps
Ahrens
Amends the SB 9 urban lot split statute to expand or clarify ministerial by-right approvals, further removing cities' authority to apply local subdivision standards or deny density-doubling splits.
AB2118 — Affordable Housing and High Road Jobs Act: Use by Right
Hoover
Amends the use-by-right approval pathway to further restrict cities to objective standards only, eliminating design review, conditional use permits, and public hearings for qualifying affordable housing projects.
SB677 — Housing Development: Transit-Oriented Development
Wiener
Amends the TOD streamlining statute to expand mandatory ministerial approval near transit, overriding local zoning, eliminating CEQA, and restricting the infrastructure conditions cities may impose.
SB908 — Housing Development: Transit-Oriented Development
Wiener
A second Wiener TOD vehicle that almost certainly preempts local zoning near transit corridors, mandates by-right approvals, and eliminates discretionary review — full text pending but author pattern is unambiguous.
AB2079 — Adaptive Reuse
Elhawary
Currently a spot bill, but flagged to create ministerial approval for adaptive reuse conversions that would override local use designations and remove city authority to evaluate compatibility or impose conditions.
AB748 — Single-Family and Multifamily Housing: Preapproved Plans
Harabedian
Mandates cities establish a preapproved plan program that processes qualifying projects outside standard design review and public hearings, imposing unfunded administrative burdens with no mechanism to recover costs.

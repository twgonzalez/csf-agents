Eight bills cross hearing thresholds in 14 days. Two are still blank. That's the point.                                                                                          
Local Control Intelligence
WEEK OF MARCH 9, 2026
 

The quietest weeks in Sacramento are often the most consequential. No votes were cast, no amendments filed — but eight bills are about to become hearable, two carry zero substantive text that could change overnight, and the architecture of this session's preemption strategy is now fully visible. What happens in the next 14 days will shape what your city is allowed to decide for the next decade.

Six days to eligible.The HAA just got sharper.your city's denial authority.AB1710 becomes eligible for its first committee hearing on March 7 — six days from now — and it targets the Housing Accountability Act directly. The bill amends Government Code §65589.5 and adds a new §65958, a combination that tightens the state's already formidable prohibition on local disapproval or conditioning of compliant housing projects. Amendments to the HAA don't just preempt local authority on a single project; they reset the legal baseline every city operates under, permanently narrowing the grounds on which a planning commission can push back, ask questions, or impose conditions. AB1751 follows five days later, eligible March 12, creating a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel — and banning cities from charging a single dollar in impact fees for new townhome residents. Eight bills in total cross their hearing-eligibility threshold within the next 14 days.

Same mechanism. Different names.Ministerial approval runs the table.no hearing, no conditions, no recourse.The bills moving this session don't operate in isolation — they share a common engine. AB2601 amends five separate streamlining statutes simultaneously, touching SB 9 duplexes, urban lot splits, and subdivision map conditions in a single bill. AB2005 expands the SB 9 parcel map provision, §66411.7, in ways that historically have enlarged the universe of parcels eligible for ministerial approval with no local discretionary review. AB2390 extends the SB 35 by-right pathway onto hazardous waste sites, removing the last local checkpoint before residents move into contaminated properties. What binds all of them is the same structural move: remove discretionary review, mandate ministerial approval, and make it illegal for your city council to ask the questions it was elected to ask.

Developers get the approval.Cities absorb the cost.existing taxpayers pay the infrastructure bill.The fiscal dimension of this session is being systematically underreported. AB1751 doesn't just remove discretionary review — it explicitly strips cities of impact fee authority, meaning water, sewer, traffic, and school capacity costs for every new townhome unit become infrastructure cost-shifting onto residents who built and paid for that capacity already. SB1014, authored by Sen. Grayson, adds two new Government Code sections that appear to restrict the offsite improvement conditions cities may impose — road dedications, utility extensions, frontage work — as a condition of development approval. SB677, Sen. Wiener's transit-oriented development bill, constrains the infrastructure conditions cities may attach to ministerially approved TOD projects near transit. The pattern is consistent: each state mandate that removes discretionary review also removes the moment at which a city can require a developer to fund the capacity their project consumes.

Blank bills don't stay blank.The window is closing fast.who California lets decide.Two spot bills — AB2079 on adaptive reuse and AB2127 on accessory dwelling units — currently carry empty summaries, and substantive preemption language can be inserted into either before their hearings are noticed. That is not an accident of legislative drafting; it is a feature of a session strategy that concentrates local control overrides in a narrow eligibility window before communities can organize a response. AB2074, Matt Haney's land use placeholder, has the same structure. The California Stewardship Fund's mission is not to stop housing — it is to ensure that the people closest to a decision retain the authority to make it. What Sacramento is building this session, bill by bill, is a statutory architecture in which that authority no longer exists. The next 14 days are the window in which that architecture either gets challenged or gets built.

 
What to Watch
AB1710 — Housing Developments: Ordinances, Policies, and Standards
Carrillo
Amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new mandate section that tightens state preemption of local denial and conditioning authority — eligible for committee hearing in six days.
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, removes all discretionary review, and bans cities from charging impact fees — making infrastructure cost-shifting to existing taxpayers mandatory by statute.
AB2601 — Streamlined Approvals and Subdivisions
Lee
Amends five separate housing streamlining statutes in a single bill, expanding ministerial approval mandates and tightening restrictions on infrastructure improvement conditions across SB 9, SB 35, and subdivision law.
AB2390 — Streamlined Approvals: Hazardous Waste Sites
Schiavo
Extends SB 35's by-right ministerial approval pathway to housing on contaminated sites, stripping cities of the discretionary review authority they use to verify adequate remediation before residents move in.
SB1014 — Development Projects: Onsite and Offsite Improvements
Grayson
Restricts the infrastructure improvement conditions — road dedications, utility extensions, frontage work — cities may impose on development approvals, shifting capacity costs directly onto existing taxpayers.
AB2005 — Urban Lot Split: Parcel Maps
Ahrens
Amends the SB 9 urban lot split statute in ways that historically expand ministerial by-right approval, removing local subdivision standards and discretionary review from qualifying parcels.
AB2118 — Affordable Housing and High Road Jobs Act: Use by Right
Hoover
Entrenches and likely expands ministerial approval under the 2022 High Road Jobs Act, eliminating public hearings, design review, and cities' ability to condition approvals on infrastructure capacity.
SB677 — Housing: Transit-Oriented Development
Wiener
Expands mandatory ministerial approval for housing near transit under §65912.156, preempting local zoning, eliminating CEQA review, and constraining infrastructure conditions cities may require.
AB1997 — Housing Approvals: Timelines and Processes
Lee
Tightens Housing Accountability Act enforcement, compresses local approval timelines, and layers new state compliance mandates onto cities with no funding offset — increasing litigation exposure for any city that applies local standards.
AB2079 — Adaptive Reuse (Spot Bill)
Elhawary
Currently a blank spot bill that is expected to create ministerial approval for adaptive reuse conversions — substantive preemption language could be inserted before the hearing is noticed.
SB908 — Housing: Transit-Oriented Development (Spot Bill)
Wiener
A second Wiener TOD vehicle, currently a spot bill, that follows the structural pattern of prior bills mandating ministerial by-right approval near transit and preempting local zoning — full text pending.

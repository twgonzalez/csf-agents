Local Control Intelligence
WEEK OF MARCH 1, 2026
 

Sacramento introduced five housing bills in the last seven days. Four of them—AB1751, AB2005, AB2118, AB2342—share the same operative mechanism: ministerial approval that eliminates your city's discretionary review and prohibits impact fees for infrastructure. This isn't a coincidence. It's a coordinated opening move in a session designed to end local zoning authority in California.

Four bills.Seven days.one target: your planning commission.Between February 9 and February 19, Sacramento introduced AB1751 (townhomes), AB2005 (urban lot splits), AB2118 (affordable housing objective standards), and AB2342 (transit-oriented development). All four amend or create ministerial approval pathways that strip cities of discretionary review—no public hearings, no design review, no conditional use permits. Projects that meet state-defined objective standards must be approved, period. Your city council doesn't get a vote. Your planning commission doesn't get to weigh in. State criteria override local zoning, and if a project checks the boxes, it's entitled.

Ministerial approval.No CEQA review.no infrastructure conditions.Here's the mechanism: ministerial approval eliminates environmental review under CEQA. It also eliminates your city's ability to condition approvals on infrastructure improvements. AB1751 explicitly prohibits cities from charging water, sewer, traffic, or school impact fees for new townhome residents. AB2005 expands SB 9's urban lot split framework, which already bans discretionary review for parcel maps. AB2118 tightens the objective standards cities may apply to affordable housing projects approved by right. AB2342 amends the transit-oriented development statute that mandates approval near transit regardless of local capacity. The pattern is identical across all four bills: state criteria in, local conditions out.

No impact fees.No improvement requirements.existing taxpayers cover the cost.When cities can't charge impact fees or require developers to fund offsite improvements—road widening, utility extensions, sewer capacity upgrades—the cost shifts to your general fund. Existing residents pay for the infrastructure new development requires. AB1751 makes this explicit: no water fees, no sewer fees, no traffic mitigation. The other three bills achieve the same result through ministerial approval—cities can't impose conditions they never get the chance to review. Your public works director knows what this means. Your finance director knows what this costs. Sacramento is betting your taxpayers don't know yet.

This is the opening.Not the exception.the session pattern you'll see all year.AB2601 amends five streamlining statutes at once. AB2433 expands density bonus entitlements. SB1014 restricts onsite and offsite improvement conditions. SB908 and SB677—both from Senator Wiener—amend transit-oriented development law to mandate more density with less local review. This week's four bills aren't outliers. They're the opening argument in a session designed to eliminate local land use authority across California. The question isn't whether Sacramento will try to override your zoning. The question is whether your city is ready to document what that override costs and who it serves.

 
What to Watch
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates statewide ministerial approval for townhomes on any residential parcel, eliminates all discretionary review, and prohibits cities from charging water, sewer, traffic, or school impact fees for new residents.
AB2005 — Urban lot split parcel maps
Ahrens
Amends SB 9's urban lot split statute to expand ministerial approval for parcel maps in single-family zones, further preempting local zoning authority and eliminating discretionary review.
AB2118 — Affordable Housing and High Road Jobs Act amendments
Hoover
Tightens objective standards cities may apply to use-by-right affordable housing projects, narrowing the only remaining local tool for shaping project design and site compatibility.
AB2342 — Transit-oriented development
Hoover
Amends TOD law to expand ministerial approval for housing near transit, overriding local zoning and eliminating CEQA review and discretionary authority.
AB2601 — Streamlined approval and subdivisions
Lee
Amends five streamlining and subdivision statutes at once to restrict local conditions and expand ministerial approvals statewide.
SB1086 — Microschools zoning mandate
Dahle
Requires cities to permit microschools as allowed uses in all zoning districts, overriding local land use decisions about where educational uses are appropriate.
SB1003 — Development project procedures
Grayson
Imposes state timelines on city review and restricts conditions cities may apply to development approvals, limiting local deliberation and mitigation authority.
SB1014 — Onsite and offsite improvements
Grayson
Restricts cities' authority to require developer-funded offsite infrastructure improvements, shifting road, utility, and frontage costs to existing taxpayers.
AB2433 — Affordable homes density bonus
Alvarez
Expands Density Bonus Law entitlements, requiring cities to grant additional density and reduced standards beyond what local zoning allows.
AB748 — Preapproved housing plans
Harabedian
Mandates cities establish preapproved plan programs that bypass discretionary review and limit local design standards for qualifying projects.
SB677 — Transit-oriented development expansion
Wiener
Expands ministerial TOD approvals near transit, eliminating CEQA, public hearings, and cities' ability to impose infrastructure conditions.
AB1710 — Housing Accountability Act amendments
Carrillo
Strengthens HAA restrictions on local disapproval or conditioning of compliant housing projects, further limiting discretionary review authority.
SB908 — Transit-oriented development
Wiener
Preempts local zoning near transit corridors, mandates ministerial approvals, and restricts cities' ability to impose development conditions.

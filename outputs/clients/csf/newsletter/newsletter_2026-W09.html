Eight bills cross eligibility thresholds in two weeks. Two have no text yet. Watch them.                                                                                          
Local Control Intelligence
WEEK OF MARCH 1, 2026
 

No votes were cast in Sacramento this week. No amendments were filed. And yet the clock is running. Eight bills cross their hearing-eligibility thresholds in the next 14 days — and two of the most consequential ones are still blank. The quiet weeks are when cities lose ground.

The calendar moved.The bills did not.that gap is the danger.Nothing advanced a legislative stage this week, and that stillness is exactly the condition Sacramento relies on. AB1710, which amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new mandate on local ordinances, becomes eligible for its first committee hearing in six days — March 7. AB1751 and AB1771 follow five days later on March 12. Eight bills in total cross their eligibility thresholds within the next 14 days, and the absence of floor activity this week means local governments have had no hearing, no vote, and no formal notice to prepare. The action window is not opening. It is closing.

Blank bills.Real deadlines approaching.preemption language, inserted without warning.The most underreported threat this week is not the bills with text — it is the ones without it. AB2127 and AB2079 are both carrying empty spot-bill summaries, and both have hearing-eligibility dates inside this 14-day window. Substantive preemption language can be inserted into either at any moment before a hearing is noticed, giving cities almost no time to analyze, respond, or organize opposition. The same pattern holds for SB1016, AB1899, AB1924, SB1090, and AB1795 — all placeholder bills, all titled in ways that could eventually encompass housing or land use authority, all sitting in the pipeline. This is how state mandate language arrives: not with fanfare, but with a committee notice that drops 72 hours before a vote.

Read AB1751 closely.Then read your water bill.existing residents absorb the cost.AB1751 is the highest-priority bill in this window and the clearest example of infrastructure cost-shifting in the current session. Authored by Sharon Quirk-Silva, it creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhome development on any residential parcel, removes discretionary review entirely, eliminates CEQA, and — critically — strips cities of all impact fee authority for new townhome residents. Water, sewer, traffic, schools: none of those costs can be charged to the developer or the project. They fall instead on the existing taxpayer base and on municipal budgets that were not sized for the load. SB1014, on the staff watchlist, compounds this problem by restricting the offsite improvement conditions cities can impose on development projects more broadly, effectively severing the link between growth and the infrastructure that must serve it. When cities cannot require developers to fund the capacity their projects consume, the bill for that capacity still arrives — just addressed to someone else.

This is not isolated.It is the session architecture.local planning authority, dismantled incrementally.Pull back from any single bill and the pattern becomes unmistakable. AB2601 amends five separate streamlining statutes simultaneously. SB677 and SB908 — both Scott Wiener transit-oriented development bills — preempt local zoning near transit corridors and mandate ministerial approvals with constrained infrastructure conditions. AB2118 tightens the objective standards cities may apply to the one approval pathway they still nominally control. AB2005 strengthens the SB9 urban lot split mandate. Each bill removes discretionary review, each one shifts infrastructure costs, and each one preempts local authority over a slightly different slice of the zoning map. Taken together, they are not a housing agenda. They are a jurisdictional transfer — from city councils and planning commissions to Sacramento — executed one Government Code section at a time. The California Stewardship Fund's position is not anti-housing. It is the same question it asks every week: who decides?

 
What to Watch
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, removes all discretionary review, and strips cities of authority to charge impact fees — shifting infrastructure costs entirely to existing taxpayers.
AB1710 — Housing Developments: Ordinances, Policies, and Standards
Carrillo
Amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new Government Code section that further restricts local conditioning authority, becoming eligible for committee in six days with no local input process in place.
SB1014 — Development Projects: Onsite and Offsite Improvements
Grayson
Restricts the infrastructure improvement conditions cities may impose on developers, eliminating the primary cost-recovery mechanism cities use when new projects strain road, utility, and frontage capacity.
SB677 — Housing Development: Transit-Oriented Development
Wiener
Amends California's TOD streamlining statute to expand mandatory ministerial approvals near transit, preempts local zoning authority, eliminates CEQA, and limits the infrastructure conditions cities may attach to covered projects.
AB2601 — Planning and Zoning: Streamlined Approval and Subdivisions
Lee
Amends five separate housing streamlining and subdivision statutes at once, compounding state preemption of local zoning across multiple approval pathways and restricting cities' ability to impose mitigation conditions.
AB2005 — Housing Developments: Urban Lot Split: Parcel Maps
Ahrens
Amends the SB9 urban lot split statute to further entrench or expand ministerial approval of lot splits in single-family zones, removing discretionary review and adding incremental infrastructure pressure with no guaranteed fee offset.
AB2118 — Affordable Housing and High Road Jobs Act: Objective Standards
Hoover
Tightens the objective standards cities may apply to use-by-right affordable housing projects, narrowing the last meaningful local tool for shaping project design under an existing preemption framework.
AB2342 — Housing Development: Transit-Oriented Development
Hoover
Amends the TOD streamlining statute to likely expand eligibility or tighten compliance obligations, reinforcing mandatory ministerial approval pathways that remove local zoning authority and CEQA review near transit.
SB908 — Housing Development: Transit-Oriented Development
Wiener
A second Wiener TOD bill that almost certainly follows the established pattern of overriding local zoning near transit corridors, mandating ministerial approvals, and constraining the infrastructure conditions cities may require.
SB1086 — Zoning: Allowed Uses: Microschools and Micro-Education Entities
Dahle
Mandates that cities permit microschools as allowed uses across zoning districts regardless of local general plan designations, preempting local authority over where institutional educational uses are appropriate.
AB748 — Single-Family and Multifamily Housing Units: Preapproved Plans
Harabedian
Forces cities to establish and administer a preapproved housing plan program that processes qualifying projects outside standard design review and public hearing processes, with no state funding to offset the administrative burden.
SB1003 — Development Projects
Grayson
Restricts the conditions cities may attach to development approvals and imposes mandatory state timelines on local review, compressing the deliberation window and limiting mitigation authority for project impacts.
AB2433 — Housing Development: Affordable Homes Bonus
Alvarez
Amends Density Bonus Law to expand entitlements cities must grant regardless of local zoning, likely broadening eligibility tiers and ministerial approval pathways that shift infrastructure costs to existing residents.
 
Before You Close This

Six days to AB1710's committee window.Contact your Assembly member today and ask them specifically where they stand on AB1710 and AB1751 — two bills that remove discretionary review and, in AB1751's case, permanently eliminate your city's impact fee authority for an entire new housing category. This isn't about stopping housing — it's about who decides. Ask the question out loud, on the record, before the hearing is noticed.

 

Hold the line.The quiet weeks are when preparation matters most. Eight bills will be eligible for committee hearings before this month ends, and two of them still carry no text — which means the preemption language hasn't been written yet, but the deadline has. Use the time. Document your city's infrastructure capacity, brief your planning director, and get your formal comment letters ready to file the moment a hearing is noticed. The session is not pausing. Neither should you.

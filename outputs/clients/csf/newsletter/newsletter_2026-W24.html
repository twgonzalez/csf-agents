Spot bills are Sacramento's sleight of hand. Here's how they work — and who's watching.                                                                                          
Local Control Intelligence
WEEK OF JUNE 8, 2026
 

The quietest legislative weeks are often the most instructive. No bills moved stage this week, no amendments dropped — but eight bills cross their committee hearing threshold in the next 14 days, and two of them carry no substantive language yet. That blank space is not an accident. It's a mechanism. Understanding it is the difference between being ready and being surprised.

Eight deadlines. Two blanks.The clock is deliberate.what gets hidden in plain sight.Nothing moved in Sacramento this week — no floor votes, no author amendments, no committee action. What the calendar shows instead is a wall of hearing-eligibility deadlines arriving in sequence: AB1710 becomes eligible for its first committee hearing in six days, on March 7, followed by AB1751 and AB1771 on March 12, with five more crossing the threshold before the two-week window closes. That compressed schedule is not unusual, but two bills on the list — AB2127 on accessory dwelling units and AB2079 on adaptive reuse — are carrying empty spot-bill summaries right now, meaning substantive preemption language could be inserted into either before a hearing is ever noticed. A quiet week in Sacramento is a good week to understand exactly how that works.

Spot bills aren't placeholders.They're loaded chambers.your city gets no warning.The spot-bill mechanism is one of Sacramento's most effective procedural tools against local control, and it operates almost entirely outside public view. A bill is introduced with a title that sounds benign — 'transitional housing bond,' 'statewide homelessness prevention strategy,' 'housing in disaster-impacted areas' — and a one-line summary that says nothing. It clears referral to the Rules Committee, the classic holding pattern, and sits. Then, before the hearing is noticed, the author drops in substantive language: a ministerial approval mandate, a fee restriction, a new preemption of local zoning. By the time the public sees a bill digest that means something, the committee hearing may be days away. SB1016, SB1090, AB1899, AB1924, and AB1795 are all in that holding pattern right now. None of them have text. Any of them could become the next bill that removes discretionary review from your planning commission.

AB1710 arrives first.The HAA gets sharper.your legal exposure, not theirs.Of the bills with actual text, AB1710 is the most immediately pressing and among the most structurally dangerous. It amends the Housing Accountability Act — already a potent state mandate — and adds a new Government Code section that further restricts cities' authority to apply local ordinances, policies, and standards to housing projects. The mechanism is asymmetric: it strengthens the anti-denial and anti-condition provisions that cities must comply with, while doing nothing to restore the infrastructure conditioning authority those same cities lose when projects qualify for streamlined approval. The result is a legal trap — cities bear the compliance burden and the litigation exposure, while the infrastructure cost-shifting to existing taxpayers continues without any replacement funding mechanism. AB1710 is eligible for hearing in six days. AB1751, which preempts local authority over townhome development on any residential parcel and explicitly strips cities of impact fee authority for new townhome residents, follows five days later.

TOD bills. ADU bills. HAA bills.Every track converges.the same city absorbs all of it.Step back from any single bill and the session pattern is unmistakable. SB677 and SB1361 press the transit-oriented development track, each amending ministerial approval statutes that already override local zoning and block infrastructure conditions near transit corridors. AB2390 tightens the SB 35 streamlined approval pathway, narrowing the objective standards cities can use to push back on qualifying projects. AB2601 compounds the damage across five separate code sections simultaneously — urban lot splits, ADUs, subdivision maps, all moving toward the same by-right floor. None of these bills are unrelated. Each one removes a tool your city would otherwise use to condition, modify, or shape a project. The cumulative effect on any single jurisdiction isn't one preemption — it's a stack of them, all landing on the same planning department, the same infrastructure budget, the same residents who will pay the carrying costs of development that was never subject to meaningful local review. That is the session pattern. It is not going to resolve itself.

 
What to Watch
AB1710 — Housing Developments: Ordinances, Policies, and Standards
Carrillo
Strengthens the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new streamlining section that preempts local authority to apply ordinances, policies, and standards to housing projects — leaving cities legally exposed and unable to condition approvals on infrastructure adequacy.
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, removes discretionary review entirely, and prohibits cities from charging impact fees — shifting all infrastructure costs to existing taxpayers.
SB677 — Housing Development: Transit-Oriented Development
Wiener
Amends the TOD streamlining statute to expand mandatory ministerial approvals near transit, preempting local zoning, eliminating CEQA review, and blocking cities from conditioning projects on infrastructure improvements.
AB2390 — Streamlined Housing Approvals: Objective Standards
Schiavo
Narrows the objective standards cities may apply under SB 35 streamlined review, further removing discretionary review authority and limiting the conditions cities can impose on qualifying projects.
AB2601 — Planning and Zoning: Streamlined Approval and Subdivisions
Lee
Expands ministerial by-right approval pathways across five separate code sections — lot splits, ADUs, SB 35 streamlining, and subdivision maps — stripping cities of discretionary review and infrastructure conditioning authority across multiple development types simultaneously.
SB1361 — Transit-Oriented Housing Developments: Local Governments
Durazo
Amends the TOD ministerial approval statute to expand mandatory by-right housing approvals near transit, overriding local zoning and blocking cities from conditioning projects on water, sewer, or traffic capacity improvements.
AB2074 — Regional Transit Hub Districts: Downtown Housing Developments
Haney
Creates a new state mandate requiring ministerial, by-right approval of downtown housing in designated transit hub districts, preempting local zoning and eliminating discretionary review in affected areas.
AB748 — Single-Family and Multifamily Housing: Preapproved Plans
Harabedian
Forces cities to establish and administer a preapproved housing plan program, creating a ministerial approval pathway that removes local design review and imposes unfunded administrative costs on planning departments.
SB1014 — Development Projects: Preliminary Estimate of Required Improvements
Grayson
Requires cities to issue preliminary improvement estimates early in the development process — a transparency measure that may functionally cap the infrastructure conditions cities can impose later, creating applicant reliance claims that constrain local authority.
SB908 — Residential Windows: Retrofitting and Building Code Compliance
Wiener
Bars cities from enforcing residential window standards stricter than state Building Code minimums, preempting local historic, energy, and design review authority and removing discretionary permit conditions for window replacement projects.
 
Before You Close This

Six days is not much runway.AB1710 becomes eligible for its first committee hearing on March 7. Contact the Assembly Housing and Community Development Committee now and put your jurisdiction on record opposing any bill that removes discretionary review, expands state mandate over local ordinances, or shifts infrastructure costs to existing residents without replacement authority. This isn't about stopping housing — it's about who decides. Your council members and planning directors should be making that call, not Sacramento.

 

Quiet weeks reveal architecture.When nothing moves, you can finally see the shape of what's being built — a session-long campaign to replace local discretionary authority with a statewide ministerial floor, bill by bill, code section by code section. The spot bills sitting empty in the Rules Committee are part of that architecture too. Stay ready.

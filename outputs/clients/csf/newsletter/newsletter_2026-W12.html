WEEK OF MARCH 16, 2026
 

The calendar is the weapon this week. No bills moved, no amendments dropped — but eight measures cross their hearing-eligibility threshold in the next 14 days, and two of them carry no substantive language at all. That's not a coincidence. That's how Sacramento resets the clock without giving anyone time to respond.

Nothing moved this week.The clock did.fourteen days to committee.The quietest legislative weeks are often the most dangerous ones to misread. No bills advanced a stage, no author amendments were filed — but that stillness masks a deadline cascade that matters enormously to your city. Eight bills cross their committee hearing-eligibility threshold within 14 days, led by AB1710 (Housing Accountability Act amendments) at six days and AB1751 (Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act) at eleven. When a bill becomes hearing-eligible, the committee can schedule it within days and move it before opposition has organized. The action isn't in Sacramento this week. It's on your calendar.

Blank bills. Real risk.Language arrives without warning.your city has no time.Two bills on the active watch list — AB2127 (accessory dwelling units) and AB2079 (adaptive reuse) — currently carry empty spot-bill summaries. That means fully operative preemption language removing discretionary review, mandating ministerial approvals, or restricting impact fees can be inserted into either bill at any moment before their hearings are noticed, giving local governments days, not weeks, to respond. The same pattern appears in the broader spot-bill pool: SB1090 is titled 'Housing: disaster impacted areas' but holds nothing but a one-line act statement; AB2074 is a Matt Haney land-use placeholder with zero operative text. Titles can be benign. The language that replaces them frequently is not. What you track today is not necessarily what gets heard next week.

AB1751 bans impact fees.Your taxpayers absorb the cost.infrastructure cost-shifting, by design.The most consequential bill approaching its hearing window is AB1751, and the fee prohibition buried in its text deserves more attention than the zoning headline gets. The bill creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhome development on any residential parcel — it preempts local authority, removes discretionary review, and strips CEQA applicability for qualifying projects. But it goes further: cities cannot charge water, sewer, traffic, or school impact fees for new townhome residents. Every connection, every pipe, every lane-mile triggered by new development becomes a state mandate your existing taxpayers fund. SB1014 (Grayson) compounds this dynamic by adding Government Code sections that appear to restrict the offsite improvement conditions cities may impose on any development project — road dedications, utility extensions, frontage work — shifting those infrastructure costs to local budgets with no replacement mechanism. These two bills read together as a coordinated fiscal dismantling of local capital recovery authority.

This is a pattern.Not a coincidence.who decides how California grows.AB2390 wants ministerial approval on hazardous waste sites, stripping the last local checkpoint before residents move onto contaminated land. AB2601 tightens the SB 35 streamlining machinery across five Government Code sections at once. SB677 and SB908 — both Wiener transit-oriented development bills — expand by-right mandates near transit corridors that already eliminate public hearings and design review. What connects them isn't the housing type or the author. It's the mechanism: every bill in this session's high-risk cluster removes discretionary review, preempts local authority, and transfers infrastructure cost-shifting obligations from developers to cities. That's not a coincidence of drafting. It's a session strategy. The California Stewardship Fund exists precisely to name that strategy before it becomes statute — and the next 14 days are where this year's fight begins.

 
What to Watch
AB1710 — Housing Developments: Ordinances, Policies, and Standards
Carrillo
Amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new mandate section that preempts local authority and removes discretionary grounds cities use to condition or deny compliant housing projects — eligible for committee in six days.
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, preempts local zoning, removes discretionary review, and bans cities from charging impact fees — shifting all infrastructure costs to existing taxpayers.
SB1014 — Development Projects: Onsite and Offsite Improvements
Grayson
Restricts the infrastructure improvement conditions cities may impose on development projects, removing local cost-recovery authority and shifting road, utility, and frontage costs to existing residents.
AB2390 — Streamlined Housing Approvals: Hazardous Waste Sites
Schiavo
Extends SB 35 ministerial approval to housing on contaminated sites, eliminating the local discretionary review cities use to verify remediation adequacy before residents move in.
AB2601 — Planning and Zoning: Housing Development: Streamlined Approval and Subdivisions
Lee
Amends five Government Code sections governing ministerial housing approvals simultaneously, deepening state preemption of local zoning and tightening restrictions on infrastructure conditions cities may require.
AB2005 — Housing Developments: Urban Lot Split: Parcel Maps
Ahrens
Amends the SB 9 urban lot split statute to likely expand ministerial by-right parcel approvals that override local zoning, subdivision standards, and infrastructure conditioning authority.
AB2118 — Affordable Housing and High Road Jobs Act: Use by Right
Hoover
Amends existing use-by-right approval law to further restrict cities to objective standards only, eliminating design review, public hearings, and conditional use permits for qualifying affordable housing projects.
SB677 — Housing Development: Transit-Oriented Development
Wiener
Amends California's TOD streamlining statute to expand mandatory ministerial approval near transit, preempting local zoning and eliminating CEQA review and public hearings for covered projects.
SB908 — Housing Development: Transit-Oriented Development
Wiener
A second Wiener TOD bill structured to override local zoning near transit corridors, mandate by-right approvals, and eliminate discretionary review — following the same preemption pattern as SB 827 and SB 50.
AB2433 — Housing Development: Affordable Homes Bonus
Alvarez
Amends Density Bonus Law to likely expand eligibility thresholds and mandated concessions, further eroding local zoning authority and compounding infrastructure capacity burdens without local mitigation tools.
AB748 — Single-Family and Multifamily Housing Units: Preapproved Plans
Harabedian
Mandates that cities create and administer a preapproved housing plan program that bypasses local design review and imposes unfunded administrative costs with no state funding offset.
 
Before You Close This

Six days. Contact your Assembly member today.AB1710 becomes hearing-eligible on March 7. Call or email your Assembly member now and ask them directly: will they support a bill that strips cities of their authority to condition housing approvals and mandate new development standards without local input? This isn't about stopping housing — it's about who decides. Your council member, your planning director, and your Assembly member all need to hear from you before that hearing is noticed.

 

Watch the calendar, not the votes.The bills that preempt local authority most aggressively rarely arrive with fanfare — they move fast through eligibility windows while attention is elsewhere, and spot bills get rewritten overnight. The work right now is tracking what's coming, not reacting to what's passed. Stay close to the watch list. The next two weeks matter.

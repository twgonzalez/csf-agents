Eight bills cross their hearing threshold in 14 days. Two of them are still blank.                                                                                          
Local Control Intelligence
WEEK OF MARCH 16, 2026
 

The Sacramento calendar is moving whether your council is watching or not. Eight bills cross their committee eligibility threshold in the next two weeks — and two of them are still spot bills, meaning the preemption language could be inserted at any moment before the hearing is noticed. This is the week to go deeper than the headlines.

Eight bills. Fourteen days.Two shells, no language yet.the window is already closing.No bills moved a legislative stage this week, and no amendments were filed — but the absence of floor action is not the same as quiet. AB1710, which amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new Government Code section that further restricts what conditions your city can impose on housing projects, becomes eligible for its first committee hearing in six days. AB1751 and AB1771 cross their thresholds five days after that. Eight bills in total hit their eligibility windows within a fortnight, and two of them — AB2127 and AB2079 — currently carry empty spot-bill summaries. Substantive preemption language can be dropped into either one at any point before the hearing is noticed, with no additional public warning required.

Same mechanism. Different bills.The architecture is deliberate.local discretion, systematically stripped.AB1710 targets the Housing Accountability Act directly, tightening the state's prohibition on local disapproval or conditioning of compliant projects — which means the grounds your planning commission uses to push back on a poorly sited development shrink further every time this section is amended. AB1751 layers a statewide ministerial pathway on top: townhomes on any residential parcel, approved by right, no discretionary review, no CEQA, no public hearing. AB2601 and AB2005 work the same seam from the subdivision side, amending the SB 9 urban lot split statute to expand the set of parcels eligible for by-right ministerial approval. SB677 and SB908 do it again along transit corridors. Each bill removes discretionary review in a different context, but the cumulative effect is a city planning department that functions less like a decision-maker and more like a rubber stamp.

Infrastructure costs stay local.Developer obligations disappear.your taxpayers absorb the difference.The fiscal angle is where the session's threat pattern becomes concrete for your finance director as much as your planning director. AB1751 explicitly bars cities from charging water, sewer, traffic, or school impact fees on qualifying townhome projects — infrastructure cost-shifting written directly into statute. SB1014, authored by Senator Grayson, adds two new Government Code sections targeting the onsite and offsite improvement conditions cities use to make developers fund the roads, utility extensions, and frontage work their projects require. Strip that authority under a state mandate and those costs don't disappear — they migrate to existing taxpayers and local capital budgets. AB2390 pushes the logic further still, extending the SB 35 ministerial approval pathway to housing projects on hazardous waste sites, preventing cities from conditioning approvals on independent cleanup verification. The infrastructure cost-shifting embedded in this session's bills isn't incidental. It is the bill.

Spot bills are the tell.Sacramento holds the pen.who decides is the whole question.The five spot bills currently on the tracker — including one titled 'Office of Youth Homelessness Prevention' and another 'Transitional housing bond' — are placeholders with a function. They reserve a legislative vehicle. Substantive preemption language can be inserted via gut-and-amend at any point before the hearing is noticed, converting an empty shell into a binding state override of local land use authority with no additional introduction period. That procedural pattern, combined with the cluster of bills tightening the HAA, expanding ministerial approval across SB 9, SB 35, the Density Bonus Law, and the TOD statutes, describes a session organized around one outcome: removing local control from every stage of the development approval process. This isn't about stopping housing — it's about who decides. Right now, Sacramento is making sure the answer is Sacramento.

 
What to Watch
AB1710 — Housing Accountability Act: ordinances, policies, and standards
Carrillo
Amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new mandate restricting the conditions and standards cities may apply to housing projects, eligible for committee in six days.
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial approval pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, eliminates discretionary review, and bars cities from charging impact fees — infrastructure cost-shifting written into statute.
AB2390 — Streamlined housing approvals: hazardous waste sites
Schiavo
Extends SB 35's ministerial approval pathway to housing on contaminated sites, removing the local discretionary review that cities use to verify cleanup before residents move in.
SB1014 — Development projects: onsite and offsite improvements
Grayson
Restricts the infrastructure improvement conditions cities may impose on developers, shifting road, utility, and frontage costs from project proponents to existing taxpayers.
AB2601 — Planning and zoning: streamlined approval and subdivisions
Lee
Amends five Government Code sections governing ministerial housing approvals and urban lot splits, each of which already preempts local zoning and removes discretionary review.
AB2005 — Housing developments: urban lot split: parcel maps
Ahrens
Amends the SB 9 urban lot split statute to expand or clarify ministerial by-right parcel approvals, stripping cities of subdivision authority and infrastructure conditioning rights.
SB677 — Housing development: transit-oriented development
Wiener
Amends the TOD streamlining statute to expand mandatory ministerial approval near transit, preempting local zoning, eliminating CEQA review, and limiting infrastructure conditions cities may require.
AB2118 — Affordable Housing and High Road Jobs Act: use by right
Hoover
Amends the use-by-right approval statute to further restrict cities to objective standards only, eliminating design review and public hearings for qualifying affordable housing projects.
AB2433 — Housing development: affordable homes bonus
Alvarez
Amends California's Density Bonus Law to expand mandatory concessions and bonus densities cities must grant, further overriding local zoning and compounding infrastructure capacity burdens.
SB908 — Housing development: transit-oriented development
Wiener
A second Wiener TOD vehicle that almost certainly follows the same structural approach as SB677 — ministerial by-right approvals near transit, local zoning preempted, discretionary review removed.

This isn't about stopping housing — it's about who decides. Watch for gut-and-amend. The next fourteen days will tell you a great deal about how the rest of this session is being run.

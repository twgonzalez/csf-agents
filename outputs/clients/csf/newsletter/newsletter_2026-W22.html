Spot bills can be loaded with preemption language before anyone notices. Here's how.                                                                                          
Local Control Intelligence
WEEK OF MAY 25, 2026
 

No votes were cast in Sacramento this week. No amendments were filed. That's not quiet — that's the calm before a procedural wave breaks. Eight bills cross their hearing-eligibility threshold in the next two weeks, and two of them are currently empty shells that can be loaded with zoning preemption language at any moment before a hearing is noticed. The time to understand the mechanism is before the text appears, not after.

The calendar moved.The bills didn't have to.eight eligibility deadlines, fourteen days.Nothing advanced a legislative stage this week, and no author filed amendments — but the most important clock in Sacramento isn't the vote count. It's the hearing-eligibility calendar. AB1710, which targets the Housing Accountability Act and directly preempts local authority to apply ordinances, policies, and standards to housing projects, becomes eligible for its first committee hearing in six days on March 7. AB1751, the Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act, follows five days later on March 12. Eight bills in total cross their eligibility thresholds within two weeks. The legislature doesn't need to move to close the window on your city's planning authority — it just needs the calendar to turn.

Two bills. No text.Any text could appear.the gut-and-amend pipeline, open.AB2127, covering accessory dwelling units, and AB2079, covering adaptive reuse, are both approaching their hearing-eligibility dates carrying empty spot-bill summaries — placeholder shells with no substantive content yet. That's not a drafting delay. That's a standard Sacramento technique: hold the vehicle, drop the payload when the political moment is right, and force a compressed committee review window on language that may have taken months to develop behind closed doors. AB2288, which targets adaptive reuse in industrial zones, and AB2342, focused on transit-oriented development, are also unfeatured bills on the high-risk watch list where the same dynamic applies. When a spot bill gets loaded, your city has days — not weeks — to respond before a hearing is noticed and the committee calendar locks.

AB1751 removes discretionary review.It also removes the invoice.infrastructure cost-shifting, written into statute.The deepest fiscal threat in the current batch isn't buried in a spot bill — it's explicit in AB1751. The Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhome development on any residential parcel, eliminating all discretionary review and CEQA applicability for qualifying projects. But the infrastructure cost-shifting provision is the clause that should be in front of every city finance director: AB1751 strips cities of impact fee authority for new townhome residents, meaning water, sewer, traffic, and school capacity costs fall entirely on existing taxpayers rather than on the development that generates them. AB2576 and SB677 apply the same fee-restriction logic to transit-oriented development corridors, and AB2433's density bonus expansion compounds the exposure by mandating ministerial-type concessions that further constrain cities' ability to require developer-funded mitigation. The pattern across all three is identical: state mandate, no local conditions, existing residents absorb the cost.

Spot bills. Eligibility windows.Ministerial tracks stacking up.a system designed to outpace local response.What this week's quiet calendar reveals is the architecture underneath the session. SB1014 requires cities to lock in preliminary improvement estimates at the pre-entitlement stage, creating a procedural record that developers can use to resist infrastructure conditions imposed later in review — a procedural preemption that doesn't announce itself as one. AB2601 extends by-right, ministerial approval across at least five code sections simultaneously, compounding existing preemptions rather than introducing a single target to oppose. AB748 mandates a preapproved plan program that removes discretionary review from qualifying projects and imposes an unfunded administrative burden on planning departments with no state offset. None of these bills operate in isolation. Together, they advance the same objective: replace local control with a statewide ministerial system that processes housing approvals faster than communities can respond. The California Stewardship Fund exists precisely because that system has a name, and your city's planning commission doesn't appear in it.

 
What to Watch
AB1710 — Housing Developments: Ordinances, Policies, and Standards
Carrillo
Expands the Housing Accountability Act to restrict local ordinances and standards cities can use to condition or deny housing projects, directly preempting local authority over development review.
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, eliminates all discretionary review and impact fee authority, and shifts infrastructure costs entirely to existing taxpayers.
AB2576 — Transit-Oriented Development
Harabedian
Amends TOD streamlining statutes to further restrict the conditions and impact fees cities may impose on qualifying transit-adjacent projects, shifting infrastructure capacity costs onto existing residents.
AB2601 — Streamlined Approval and Subdivisions
Lee
Expands ministerial, by-right approval across five code sections — urban lot splits, ADUs, SB 35 streamlining, and subdivision maps — stripping discretionary review and infrastructure conditioning authority simultaneously.
SB677 — Housing Development: Transit-Oriented Development
Wiener
Strengthens mandatory ministerial approval for transit-adjacent housing under Government Code §65912.156, preempting local zoning, eliminating CEQA review, and constraining infrastructure conditions cities may require.
AB2074 — Regional Transit Hub Districts: Downtown Housing
Haney
Creates a new state-mandated 'regional transit hub district' framework that compels by-right, ministerial approval of downtown housing near transit, overriding local design review and CEQA applicability.
AB2118 — Affordable Housing and High Road Jobs Act: Use by Right
Hoover
Expands ministerial approval and objective-standards-only review under the High Road Jobs Act, removing discretionary authority and constraining cities' ability to require infrastructure improvements as a condition of approval.
SB1014 — Development Projects: Preliminary Estimate of Required Improvements
Grayson
Requires cities to provide early pre-entitlement improvement estimates that developers can use to legally resist additional infrastructure conditions later in review, effectively capping local mitigation authority.
AB2433 — Housing Development: Density Bonus
Alvarez
Expands Density Bonus Law to compel cities to approve density levels and grant developer concessions beyond what local zoning allows, removing discretionary review and straining infrastructure without required developer mitigation.
AB748 — Single-Family and Multifamily Housing: Preapproved Plans
Harabedian
Mandates a local preapproved plan program that removes discretionary review from qualifying projects and imposes an unfunded administrative state mandate on planning departments with no funding offset.
 
Before You Close This

Six days is not a planning horizon.Before March 7, contact your Assembly member and ask them directly where they stand on AB1710 — specifically whether they support restricting the local ordinances and standards cities can apply to housing projects. Forward this issue to your planning director and city attorney today; they need to know that spot bills AB2127 and AB2079 could receive substantive preemption language before a hearing is noticed, leaving days to respond. This isn't about stopping housing — it's about who decides how it gets built, who pays for the infrastructure it requires, and whether your community has any voice in the answer.

 

The calendar is the strategy.Sacramento doesn't need a dramatic floor vote to preempt your city's planning authority — it needs eligibility windows, spot bill vehicles, and a compressed committee calendar that outpaces local response.

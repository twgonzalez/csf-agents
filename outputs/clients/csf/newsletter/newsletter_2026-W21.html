Empty spot bills can be loaded with preemption language overnight. Here's what to watch.                                                                                          
Local Control Intelligence
WEEK OF MAY 18, 2026
 

Sacramento didn't move anything forward this week — but the calendar did. Eight bills cross their committee hearing-eligibility threshold in the next 14 days, and two of them are still blank spot bills that could receive sweeping preemption language before anyone notices. A quiet week in the Capitol is not the same thing as a safe week for your city.

Nothing passed. Deadlines advanced.Eight bills. Fourteen days.the window is closing fast.No bills moved a legislative stage this week, and no author amendments were filed — but that stillness is a function of the calendar, not a pause in the pressure. AB1710, which amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new Government Code section that directly restricts what local ordinances, policies, and standards cities may apply to housing developments, becomes eligible for its first committee hearing in six days, on March 7. AB1751 and AB1771 follow five days later. In a session this dense with high-risk preemption language, an empty action calendar isn't relief — it's the gap between when a bill is loaded and when it fires.

Spot bills. No content yet.Any language. Any day.your city won't see it coming.Two bills on the near-term eligibility list — AB2127 and AB2079 — currently carry empty spot-bill summaries, meaning their authors can insert substantive preemption language at any moment before a hearing is noticed. That's not a hypothetical: gut-and-amend is the preferred mechanism for moving aggressive housing preemption language through committees before opposition can organize. Elsewhere on the spot-bill roster, SB1090 is titled 'Housing: disaster impacted areas' but contains nothing beyond the printer stage — a vessel that could easily receive by-right approval mandates for fire-affected parcels before local governments even know the language exists. The titles signal intent. The empty shells provide cover.

AB2390 builds on SB 35.Hazardous sites. Ministerial approval.public health review, gone.While AB1751 draws the most attention for its infrastructure cost-shifting — the bill explicitly prohibits cities from charging water, sewer, traffic, or school impact fees for new townhome residents, shifting those costs entirely to existing taxpayers — AB2390 may carry the session's most underreported risk. The bill expands the SB 35 ministerial approval pathway to housing projects on hazardous waste sites, removing discretionary review authority over the one category of property where community-specific environmental conditions matter most. Cities currently use that discretionary review to verify remediation adequacy before residents move in. AB2390 eliminates that checkpoint and prevents cities from conditioning or delaying approvals while cleanup is independently verified. There is no replacement mechanism. The state mandate runs straight through the fence line of contaminated ground.

Pattern, not coincidence.Every pathway. Every site type.local control is the target.Step back from any single bill and the architecture of this session becomes clear. AB2601 expands ministerial approval across five separate code sections simultaneously — urban lot splits, ADUs, SB 35-style streamlining, lot splits, and subdivision maps — foreclosing local discretion at every stage of the development pipeline. SB677 and AB2576 close off the transit corridor. AB2074 creates 'regional transit hub districts' where cities lose authority over density, height, use, and design review by statute. AB2433 expands the Density Bonus Law to compel additional concessions that local governments must grant regardless of infrastructure capacity. None of these bills conflict with each other. They are designed to interlock — each one closing off a different avenue cities might use to preserve meaningful local control over how their communities grow. This isn't about stopping housing. It's about who decides.

 
What to Watch
AB1710 — Housing Developments: Ordinances, Policies, and Standards
Carrillo
Amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds Government Code §65958 to restrict what local ordinances, policies, and standards cities may apply to housing projects — eligible for committee hearing in six days.
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, eliminates all discretionary review, and bans cities from charging impact fees — shifting infrastructure costs entirely to existing taxpayers.
AB2390 — Streamlined Housing Approvals: Hazardous Waste Sites
Schiavo
Extends SB 35 ministerial approval to housing on contaminated sites, stripping cities of the discretionary review they use to verify cleanup before residents move in.
AB2601 — Streamlined Approval and Subdivisions
Lee
Expands by-right ministerial approval across five code sections covering lot splits, ADUs, SB 35 streamlining, and subdivision maps, removing local discretion at every stage of the development pipeline.
AB2576 — Transit-Oriented Development
Harabedian
Amends the TOD statute to mandate ministerial approval near transit corridors, preempts local zoning, eliminates CEQA review, and restricts infrastructure conditions cities may impose on qualifying projects.
AB2074 — Regional Transit Hub Districts
Haney
Creates state-designated 'regional transit hub districts' where cities lose authority over density, height, use, and design review under a mandatory by-right approval framework.
SB677 — Housing Development: Transit-Oriented Development
Wiener
Strengthens mandatory ministerial approval for TOD projects under Government Code §65912.156, overriding local zoning and eliminating public hearings and environmental review for qualifying developments.
AB2433 — Housing Development: Density Bonus
Alvarez
Expands Density Bonus Law to compel cities to grant additional density, incentives, and waivers beyond local zoning — regardless of infrastructure capacity or community planning priorities.
AB1621 — Postentitlement Phase Permits: Housing Accountability Act
Wilson
Dual amendments to the HAA and postentitlement permit law stack state override authority across both the entitlement and permit-issuance phases, compressing every window cities have to raise infrastructure or safety concerns.
SB1014 — Development Projects: Preliminary Estimate of Required Improvements
Grayson
Requires early disclosure of infrastructure improvement estimates — and in practice pressures cities to honor those estimates, limiting their flexibility to impose full mitigation conditions discovered later in project review.
AB748 — Single-Family and Multifamily Housing: Preapproved Plans
Harabedian
Mandates that cities build and administer a preapproved plan program that removes discretionary review for qualifying projects — an unfunded state mandate with no mechanism to preserve community design input.

Local Control Intelligence
WEEK OF MARCH 23, 2026
 

Sacramento didn't move a single bill this week — and that's exactly the problem. Quiet calendars are where the real work happens: deadlines accumulating, spot bills sitting open, and two blank-page bills that could become your city's next zoning nightmare before the ink is dry. Eight bills cross their committee eligibility threshold in the next 14 days. The clock is already running.

Nothing moved this week.Eight deadlines didn't pause.the quiet is the strategy.No votes, no amendments, no stage changes — by any surface reading, this was a slow week in Sacramento. It wasn't. AB1710, which amends the Housing Accountability Act to further restrict what conditions cities may impose on housing approvals, becomes eligible for its first committee hearing in six days, on March 7. AB1751, the Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act, follows five days later. Eight bills in total cross their hearing-eligibility threshold within the next 14 days. Legislative calendars compress fastest when nobody is watching them.

Spot bills sit open.Preemption language drops anytime.your city has no warning.Two bills on the immediate watch list — AB2127 and AB2079 — are carrying empty summaries right now, meaning substantive preemption language can be inserted into either vehicle at any moment before hearings are noticed. That's not a hypothetical. It is the standard gut-and-amend mechanic Sacramento uses to move policy faster than local governments can respond. Five additional spot bills tracked this session — including SB1090, titled 'Housing: disaster impacted areas,' and AB1924, a 'Statewide homelessness prevention strategy' — carry nothing beyond the printer stage today. Each one is a blank check waiting to be written against local control.

Infrastructure costs have no author.Your taxpayers do.the fiscal mechanism hiding in plain sight.The fiscal architecture running beneath this week's watch list is more consistent than any individual bill title suggests. AB1751 removes impact fee authority entirely for new townhome development — water, sewer, traffic, schools — so infrastructure costs shift to existing taxpayers the moment a project qualifies. SB1014, Senator Grayson's development improvements bill, restricts the onsite and offsite improvement conditions cities may impose as a condition of approval, cutting off the second channel cities use to recover capacity costs triggered by new projects. AB2390 extends ministerial approval under SB 35 to housing on hazardous waste sites, blocking cities from conditioning approvals on independent remediation verification and shifting liability onto future residents. These bills don't reference each other. They don't need to. The infrastructure cost-shifting is the common output regardless of the stated rationale.

One session. One architecture.Local authority, systematically removed.who decides is always the question.Step back from any single bill and what this session is building becomes visible: AB2601 tightens ministerial approval across multiple existing streamlining statutes simultaneously; SB677 and SB908 extend by-right TOD pathways near transit; AB2074 creates a new by-right corridor for downtown housing in regional transit hub districts with an appropriation attached; AB2005 expands urban lot splits under SB 9. None of these bills preempts local authority by accident — each one removes discretionary review through a specific statutory mechanism, forecloses infrastructure conditioning, and mandates state mandate compliance on a timeline cities didn't set. The California Stewardship Fund exists for exactly this moment: to name the architecture before it becomes permanent, and to make sure every council member and planning commissioner who reads this newsletter understands that the fight isn't over housing. It's over who decides.

 
What to Watch
AB1710 — Housing Developments: Ordinances, Policies, and Standards
Carrillo
Amends the Housing Accountability Act to further restrict the conditions cities may apply to housing approvals, with committee eligibility arriving in six days on March 7.
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, eliminates all discretionary review and CEQA, and strips cities of impact fee authority — shifting all infrastructure costs to existing taxpayers.
AB2390 — Streamlined Housing Approvals: Hazardous Waste Sites
Schiavo
Extends SB 35 ministerial approval — which already removes discretionary review and CEQA — to housing on contaminated sites, eliminating the last local checkpoint for remediation verification before residents move in.
SB1014 — Development Projects: Onsite and Offsite Improvements
Grayson
Restricts the infrastructure improvement conditions cities may require as a condition of approval, directly limiting local cost-recovery authority and shifting capacity burdens onto existing residents.
AB2601 — Streamlined Approval and Subdivisions
Lee
Amends five separate streamlining and subdivision statutes simultaneously, expanding ministerial approval mandates and tightening restrictions on the infrastructure conditions cities may impose across multiple development types.
AB2074 — Regional Transit Hub Districts: Downtown Housing
Haney
Creates a new by-right approval pathway for downtown housing in regional transit hub districts, preempting local zoning and removing discretionary review while carrying a state appropriation.
SB677 — Housing Development: Transit-Oriented Development
Wiener
Amends California's TOD streamlining statute to expand or strengthen mandatory ministerial approval near transit, overriding local zoning and eliminating public hearings for covered projects.
SB908 — Housing Development: Transit-Oriented Development
Wiener
A second Wiener TOD vehicle that follows the same structural pattern as SB 677 — by-right preemption of local zoning near transit corridors — with full bill text still pending.
AB2005 — Housing Developments: Urban Lot Split: Parcel Maps
Ahrens
Amends the SB 9 urban lot split statute to expand ministerial by-right parcel approvals, further stripping cities of subdivision authority and discretionary review over density increases on single-family parcels.
AB2433 — Housing Development: Affordable Homes Bonus
Alvarez
Amends California's Density Bonus Law to expand eligibility thresholds or increase mandated concessions, deepening state preemption of local zoning and eliminating remaining discretionary review for qualifying projects.
AB748 — Single-Family and Multifamily Housing: Preapproved Plans
Harabedian
Forces cities to establish a preapproved plan program that removes discretionary review for qualifying projects and imposes unfunded administrative costs on local planning departments with no state funding offset.

WEEK OF JUNE 15, 2026
 

The legislature didn't move a single bill this week — and that's exactly what makes this moment dangerous. Eight bills cross committee eligibility thresholds in the next 14 days, two of them carrying no substantive language at all. Quiet weeks aren't rest periods in Sacramento. They're loading screens.

Nothing moved this week.Eight deadlines did.the window closes in six days.AB1710 becomes eligible for its first committee hearing on March 7 — six days from now. AB1751 and AB1771 follow on March 12. Eight bills in total cross hearing-eligibility thresholds within the next 14 days, and the legislature's quiet week didn't pause that clock. What it did do is compress your response window further. When Sacramento is still, local governments tend to go still too. That asymmetry is not accidental.

Blank bills. Real deadlines.Language drops without warning.your planning department won't see it coming.AB2127 and AB2079 are currently carrying empty spot-bill summaries — placeholder legislation with no substantive preemption language yet, but active hearing-eligibility timelines. That's how gut-and-amend works at its most aggressive: a bill sits inert until the day before a hearing is noticed, at which point new language is dropped that removes discretionary review, preempts local authority, or eliminates impact fee power. Two other spot bills on the extended watch list — SB1016 and SB1090 — are holding the same position. The title sounds innocuous; the content will not be.

AB1710 amends the HAA.That's the whole game.your conditions of approval, gone.AB1710 doesn't just touch housing law — it amends Section 65589.5, the Housing Accountability Act, and adds a brand-new Section 65958 to the Government Code. HAA amendments are the preferred instrument for expanding state mandate authority because they don't need to invent new law; they extend the reach of a preemption framework that already has litigation teeth. In practice, that means cities lose another layer of their ability to condition project approvals on infrastructure capacity. AB1751 does the same thing from a different angle — it creates a ministerial townhome pathway that eliminates impact fee authority entirely, shifting infrastructure cost-shifting directly onto existing residents. Both bills score 4-of-4 on local control risk criteria. Both are eligible for committee in 11 days or fewer.

This isn't one bill.It's an architecture.who decides how California grows.Look at the full eligibility queue and a structural logic emerges. AB2390 tightens the SB 35 ministerial pathway so cities have fewer grounds to push back on streamlined projects. AB2433 expands Density Bonus Law concessions cities must grant. AB2074 creates by-right approval zones around regional transit hubs, eliminating conditional use review in the corridors where development pressure is already highest. SB677 compresses the Subdivision Map Act appeal timeline. No single bill is the threat. The threat is the cumulative erosion — each law narrowing what local control means until the phrase is ceremonial. The California Stewardship Fund tracks this architecture bill by bill because the people who write it are counting on you to see only the individual pieces.

 
What to Watch
AB1710 — Housing Ordinances, Policies, and Standards
Carrillo
Amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new Government Code section that will almost certainly expand ministerial approvals and strip cities of their remaining conditions-of-approval authority — eligible for committee in six days.
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, removes all discretionary review, and eliminates city authority to charge impact fees — infrastructure costs shift entirely to existing taxpayers.
AB2390 — Streamlined Approvals: Objective Standards
Schiavo
Amends SB 35's ministerial approval statute to narrow the grounds on which cities can modify or push back on qualifying projects, directly eroding what little discretionary review remains under streamlined housing law.
AB2074 — Regional Transit Hub Districts
Haney
Creates a new Government Code section establishing transit hub districts where downtown housing gets by-right approval, preempting local zoning and eliminating discretionary review in the highest-pressure development corridors in the state.
SB677 — Housing Approvals: Subdivision Map Act
Wiener
Amends the Housing Accountability Act and the Subdivision Map Act to restrict local denial authority and compress appeal timelines, limiting cities' ability to condition or contest project approvals.
AB748 — Preapproved Housing Plans
Harabedian
Forces cities to build and administer a preapproved plan program, mandating ministerial approval for conforming projects and eliminating discretionary design review — with no state funding to cover the compliance burden.
AB2433 — Density Bonus Law Amendment
Alvarez
Expands California's Density Bonus Law to require cities to grant additional concessions and incentives to qualifying projects, further eroding local authority over height, setbacks, parking, and unit counts.
SB1367 — Zoning: Detention Facilities
Cervantes
Adds Government Code §65850.05 to strip cities of zoning authority over detention facility siting, removing conditional use review and eliminating the ability to require infrastructure improvements as conditions of approval.
SB1014 — Preliminary Estimate: Required Improvements
Grayson
Requires cities to produce binding preliminary infrastructure improvement estimates at the pre-application stage — a new unfunded mandate that could create applicant reliance claims limiting what cities can later require of developers.
AB2415 — Transit-Oriented Housing: Alternative Plans
Hoover
Amends the TOD streamlining statute to create an alternative plans pathway that overrides local zoning and limits conditioning authority for qualifying housing projects near transit.

Spot bills can swallow preemption language overnight. Here's what to watch.                                                                                          
Local Control Intelligence
WEEK OF MARCH 30, 2026
 

Sacramento didn't move this week — but the calendar did. Eight bills cross their first committee hearing threshold in the next 14 days, and two of them carry no substantive language yet, meaning the actual preemption text could drop at any moment before a hearing is noticed. A quiet week in the Capitol is not a safe week for your city.

The calendar moved.The bills didn't.that gap is the threat.No bills advanced a legislative stage this week, and no author amendments were filed — but eight bills cross their committee hearing eligibility threshold within the next 14 days, starting with AB1710 on March 7. That proximity matters because eligibility and action can collapse quickly: a bill becomes hearable, a committee chair slots it, a vote happens, and cities that weren't watching miss their window to submit comment letters or mobilize planning directors. AB1710 amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new Government Code section — a combination that has historically preempts local authority to deny or condition projects based on anything Sacramento doesn't recognize as an objective standard. Six days is not much runway.

Blank bills.Real deadlines approaching.your zoning code, rewritten overnight.The more underappreciated risk this week sits inside two spot bills: AB2127 and AB2079, both carrying empty summaries with hearing eligibility dates closing in. A spot bill is a legislative placeholder — introduced with a title and a one-line description, substantive preemption language inserted later, sometimes 24 hours before a committee hearing is noticed. AB2079 is titled 'adaptive reuse,' a category that already carries strong preemption signals based on companion bills like AB2288, which targets industrial zones specifically. AB2127 covers accessory dwelling units, a statutory area Sacramento has amended aggressively for five consecutive sessions. Either vehicle could absorb language that removes discretionary review, eliminates infrastructure conditions, or creates a new ministerial pathway — and the public record won't show it until the amendment drops.

Fees stripped.Infrastructure costs stay.your existing taxpayers absorb it.The fiscal mechanism running beneath this session's bill cluster is consistent and it is not accidental. AB1751 explicitly bars cities from charging impact fees for new townhome residents — water, sewer, traffic, schools — while SB1014 goes further by restricting the onsite and offsite improvement conditions cities may impose on any development project, shifting road dedications, utility extensions, and frontage work off developer ledgers and onto municipal budgets. AB2390 extends this logic to hazardous waste sites, where ministerial approval under the SB35 pathway would prevent cities from conditioning approval on independent remediation verification. Taken together, these bills constitute a structural infrastructure cost-shifting program: the state mandate forces the approval, strips the fee authority, and leaves existing residents to fund the capacity strain generated by projects their city had no meaningful power to shape.

One session.Dozens of preemption vectors.the pattern is the strategy.What this week's quiet calendar reveals, when you map it against the full watch list, is that the threat to local control in 2026 isn't concentrated in one landmark bill — it is distributed across a dense field of narrowly targeted amendments, each one chipping a specific tool out of the local planning toolkit. SB677 targets transit corridors. AB2074 targets downtown transit hubs. AB2005 tightens urban lot split ministerial approvals. AB2601 amends five separate streamlining statutes simultaneously. No single bill is the main event; the cumulative effect is. Cities that engage on AB1751 and ignore SB1014 will find they've defended one wall while another was removed. The California Stewardship Fund's position is unchanged: this isn't about stopping housing — it's about who decides how communities grow, and right now Sacramento is systematically removing that decision from the people closest to it.

 
What to Watch
AB1710 — Housing Developments: Ordinances, Policies, and Standards
Carrillo
Amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new streamlining section that further restricts cities' ability to deny or condition housing projects — eligible for its first committee hearing in six days.
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, eliminates all discretionary review, and bars cities from charging impact fees for new townhome residents — infrastructure costs shift entirely to existing taxpayers.
SB1014 — Development Projects: Onsite and Offsite Improvements
Grayson
Restricts the improvement conditions cities may impose on developers — roads, utilities, frontage — directly removing the primary mechanism cities use to recover infrastructure costs triggered by new development.
AB2390 — Streamlined Housing Approvals: Hazardous Waste Sites
Schiavo
Extends the SB35 ministerial approval pathway to housing on contaminated sites, stripping cities of the discretionary review they use to verify remediation adequacy before residents move in.
AB2601 — Planning and Zoning: Housing Development: Streamlined Approval and Subdivisions
Lee
Amends five separate Government Code sections governing ministerial housing approvals simultaneously, expanding state preemption across urban lot splits, SB9 duplexes, SB35 streamlining, and subdivision conditions in a single bill.
AB2074 — Regional Transit Hub Districts: Downtown Housing Developments
Haney
Creates a new ministerial by-right approval pathway for downtown transit hub developments that explicitly preempts local zoning, eliminates design review and CEQA, and shifts infrastructure obligations onto city budgets.
AB2005 — Housing Developments: Urban Lot Split: Parcel Maps
Ahrens
Amends the SB9 urban lot split statute to further constrain cities' authority to deny or condition ministerial parcel splits, mandating density increases beyond what local general plans would choose.
SB677 — Housing Development: Transit-Oriented Development
Wiener
Amends California's TOD streamlining statute to expand or strengthen mandatory ministerial approval near transit, overriding local zoning and eliminating discretionary review and public hearings for qualifying projects.
AB2433 — Housing Development: Density Bonus
Alvarez
Amends the Density Bonus Law to expand state mandates compelling cities to approve density increases and development concessions beyond what local zoning or community planning would permit.
AB748 — Single-Family and Multifamily Housing Units: Preapproved Plans
Harabedian
Forces cities to establish and administer a preapproved housing plan program that bypasses local design review and public hearings while imposing unfunded administrative costs on local planning departments.
 
Before You Close This

Six days. One letter. Send it now.AB1710 becomes eligible for its first committee hearing on March 7 — contact your Assembly member and the Housing and Community Development Committee today and ask them directly where they stand on a bill that further removes discretionary review from your city's planning process. This isn't about stopping housing — it's about who decides. Your planning director has the standing to submit formal comment; that letter needs to be drafted before the weekend.

 

Patience is a tactic too.A quiet week in the Capitol is Sacramento's calendar working in its favor — eligibility windows close, spot bills stay blank, and cities that aren't watching miss the moment to engage. The watch list above is not theoretical; each bill on it has a hearing date, and most have no offsetting mechanism to protect local infrastructure authority or preserve meaningful community input. Track the spot bills. Draft the comment letters. The session is not pausing — it is loading.

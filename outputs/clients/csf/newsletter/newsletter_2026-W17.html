WEEK OF APRIL 20, 2026
 

Sacramento didn't move a single bill this week. That's the point. The quiet before a committee calendar is when blank placeholder bills get loaded with preemption language — and by the time the hearing is noticed, cities have days to respond. Eight bills cross their eligibility threshold in the next two weeks. Two of them have no substantive text yet.

No votes. No amendments.But the clock is running.and two bills are still blank.This was a procedurally quiet week — no bills advanced a legislative stage, no author amendments were filed. Don't mistake that for stillness. AB1710 becomes eligible for its first committee hearing in six days, on March 7. AB1751 and AB1771 follow five days later. Eight bills in total cross their hearing-eligibility threshold within the next 14 days. And two of the most closely watched vehicles — AB2127 on accessory dwelling units and AB2079 on adaptive reuse — currently carry empty spot-bill summaries, meaning substantive preemption language can be inserted into either at any moment before a hearing is noticed. By the time your planning director sees the text, the window to respond may already be closing.

Different bill numbers.Same mechanical result.your zoning code, overwritten by Sacramento.The bills converging on the committee calendar this month aren't independent policy experiments — they're variations on a single architecture. AB1751 creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, scoring the maximum risk rating on all four criteria: it preempts local authority, removes discretionary review, mandates approval, and strips impact fee authority entirely. AB1710 amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new Government Code section that tightens the standard cities must meet to deny or reduce projects, compounding legal exposure for any local government that tries to apply its own planning standards. SB677 expands California's transit-oriented development streamlining statute to mandate ministerial approval near transit, eliminating CEQA review, public hearings, and design conditions for qualifying projects. Each bill operates on a different surface — townhomes, HAA compliance, transit proximity — but each one removes a layer of the discretionary review that gives communities meaningful input over what gets built and where.

Not just zoning authority.Not just design review.your city's ability to pay for growth.The fiscal dimension of this package is what most coverage misses. AB1751 explicitly prohibits cities from charging water, sewer, traffic, or school impact fees for new townhome residents — infrastructure cost-shifting written directly into the statute. AB2433 expands the Density Bonus Law, which by design compels ministerial approvals and limits the conditions cities may impose, meaning utility and capacity improvements cities would normally require as part of project approval simply don't happen — and existing ratepayers absorb the cost. SB1014, authored by Tim Grayson, adds a new wrinkle: it requires cities to produce formal preliminary estimates of required infrastructure improvements early in the development review process, and those estimates could be weaponized in litigation to challenge any final conditions that exceed the initial figure. The transparency rationale is reasonable; the mechanism caps local infrastructure conditioning authority at the moment cities know the least about a project. Taken together, this session's bills don't just strip zoning power — they defund the fiscal tools cities use to make growth pay for itself.

Spot bills change overnight.Eligibility windows don't.this is how preemption gets through.The procedural pattern running through this session deserves its own map. A bill is introduced with a one-line placeholder summary — 'an act relating to housing' — and parked at the printer stage. It draws no attention, triggers no opposition research, generates no council resolutions. Then, days before the hearing eligibility date, the author drops in substantive language. The bill moves to committee. Cities have a week, sometimes less, to read the new text, brief their councils, coordinate with peer cities, and file opposition. Bills like SB1003, AB2288, AB2295, AB2342, and AB2390 are currently in various stages of this holding pattern, and none has yet generated the scrutiny it may warrant. This is not an accident of the legislative calendar. It is the mechanism by which the preemption of local control advances — not through a single dramatic vote, but through the accumulated pressure of bills that arrive faster than cities can respond to them. The role of your council, your planning commission, and your advocacy network is to close that gap.

 
What to Watch
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial approval pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, preempts local authority over zoning and design, and bars cities from collecting impact fees — shifting all infrastructure costs to existing taxpayers.
AB1710 — Housing Developments: Ordinances, Policies, and Standards
Carrillo
Amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new Government Code section that removes discretionary review for qualifying housing projects, tightening the legal noose on any city that attempts to apply its own planning standards.
SB677 — Housing Development: Transit-Oriented Development
Wiener
Expands the state's transit-oriented development streamlining statute to mandate ministerial, by-right approval near transit, eliminating CEQA, public hearings, and the infrastructure conditions cities rely on to make dense development financially viable for existing residents.
AB2074 — Regional Transit Hub Districts: Downtown Housing Developments
Haney
Creates a new Government Code section that imposes ministerial approval mandates in designated transit hub districts, overriding local zoning and blocking cities from conditioning approvals on utility and frontage improvements.
AB2433 — Housing Development: Density Bonus
Alvarez
Expands the Density Bonus Law to require cities to grant greater density increases and concessions to a broader set of projects, stripping discretionary review and increasing unit counts cities cannot condition on infrastructure capacity improvements.
SB1014 — Development Projects: Preliminary Estimate of Required Improvements
Grayson
Requires cities to produce formal infrastructure improvement estimates early in project review, creating an unfunded state mandate and a litigation lever that could cap a city's infrastructure conditioning authority before full review is complete.
AB748 — Single-Family and Multifamily Housing: Preapproved Plans
Harabedian
Forces cities to establish and administer a preapproved housing plan program that removes discretionary review for qualifying projects and imposes unfunded administrative costs with no mechanism to recover expenses or preserve community input.
SB908 — Residential Windows: Retrofitting: California Energy Code Compliance
Wiener
Adds Government Code preemption language that restricts cities from enforcing local design standards on window retrofits, threatening historic district protections and creating an unfunded ministerial review obligation with no state reimbursement.
 
Before You Close This

Brief your council on the spot bill risk: two bills on the active calendar, AB2127 and AB2079, carry no substantive text yet and could be rewritten before their hearings are noticed. This isn't about stopping housing — it's about who decides. Forward this issue to your city attorney and planning director today.

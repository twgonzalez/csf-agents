Spot bills are Sacramento's oldest trick. Here's how to watch for the switch.                                                                                          
Local Control Intelligence
WEEK OF MAY 11, 2026
 

The legislature didn't move this week — and that's exactly when you need to be paying attention. Eight bills cross their committee hearing-eligibility threshold in the next 14 days, and two of them carry no substantive language yet. When a bill is blank, it isn't dormant. It's a vessel waiting to be filled.

Quiet week. Loud clock.Eight bills. Fourteen days.the window closes fast.No bills advanced a legislative stage this week and no author amendments were filed — but the action calendar is anything but idle. AB1710, which amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new preemption provision under Government Code §65958, becomes eligible for its first committee hearing in six days on March 7. AB1751, the Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act, follows five days later on March 12. Eight bills in total cross their eligibility threshold within a fortnight. The absence of floor drama this week is not a reprieve — it is the calm before a compressed hearing schedule where decisions get made before most city councils have even been briefed.

Blank bills. Real threat.Language drops without warning.your planning staff won't see it coming.The most underreported risk in the current session isn't the bills everyone is already watching — it's the ones that haven't shown their hand yet. AB2127 and AB2079 are both approaching their hearing-eligibility windows carrying empty spot-bill summaries, meaning substantive preemption language can be inserted into either at any moment before a hearing is noticed. This is gut-and-amend territory: a bill titled one thing becomes something else entirely, often with less than a week between text publication and committee vote. Sacramento has refined this procedure into an art form. Your staff's job is to catch the switch before the gavel falls. That requires monitoring the chaptered text of every placeholder on the eligibility calendar — not just the bills with fully drafted language.

Infrastructure costs. No offset.Cities absorb what developers won't.your existing taxpayers are the backstop.The fiscal thread running through this session's preemption bills is infrastructure cost-shifting, and it compounds with every new ministerial approval pathway Sacramento creates. AB1751 preempts local authority to charge water, sewer, and traffic impact fees on townhome projects — those costs don't disappear, they redistribute onto existing residents and strained municipal budgets. AB2390 extends SB 35's by-right approval framework to housing on hazardous waste sites, stripping cities of the discretionary review tools they use to require remediation verification before occupancy. AB2074 creates a regional transit hub district overlay that forces ministerial approval for downtown housing, preventing cities from conditioning projects on the utility and frontage improvements those projects will demonstrably require. In each case, the state mandate arrives without a funding mechanism. The infrastructure gap is local government's problem to solve.

One session. One strategy.Stack enough preemptions. Local control collapses.the pattern is the point.Considered individually, any one of these bills can be framed as a housing production tool. Considered together — AB1710 tightening the Housing Accountability Act, SB677 and AB2576 expanding transit-oriented development mandates, AB2433 expanding Density Bonus Law entitlements, AB1621 compressing the post-entitlement permit window — they form a coherent architecture for removing local authority from every stage of the development process: entitlement, design, permitting, and infrastructure conditioning. This isn't an accident of legislative coincidence. Each bill removes discretionary review at a different chokepoint. The cumulative result is a planning system where the state sets the terms and cities absorb the costs. California Stewardship Fund's position has never been anti-housing — it has been, consistently, that the question of how communities grow belongs to the people who live in them. That principle is what this session is testing.

 
What to Watch
AB1710 — Housing Developments: Ordinances, Policies, and Standards
Carrillo
Amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds §65958 to further preempt local authority to apply community standards to qualifying housing projects, eligible for committee hearing in six days.
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, removes all discretionary review, and eliminates local impact fee authority — shifting infrastructure costs entirely to existing taxpayers.
AB2390 — Streamlined Housing Approvals: Hazardous Waste Sites
Schiavo
Extends SB 35's by-right ministerial approval to housing on contaminated sites, stripping cities of the discretionary review authority they use to verify remediation before residents move in.
AB2074 — Regional Transit Hub Districts: Downtown Housing
Haney
Creates a state-mandated transit hub district overlay that forces ministerial approval for downtown housing near major transit stations, eliminating CEQA, design review, and infrastructure conditioning authority.
AB2576 — Transit-Oriented Development
Harabedian
Amends the TOD streamlining statute to expand or deepen state preemption of local zoning and removes discretionary review for housing near transit without preserving any infrastructure cost-recovery mechanism.
SB677 — Housing Development: Transit-Oriented Development
Wiener
Strengthens mandatory ministerial approval for housing near transit under §65912.156, overriding local zoning, eliminating public hearings, and restricting the infrastructure conditions cities may impose.
AB1621 — Postentitlement Phase Permits: Housing Accountability Act
Wilson
Stacks HAA and postentitlement permit amendments to compress every stage of local review after entitlement, leaving cities with near-zero authority to raise infrastructure or safety concerns before permits issue.
AB2433 — Housing Development: Density Bonus
Alvarez
Amends the Density Bonus Law to expand the state preemption framework that already compels cities to approve density beyond local zoning — full text not yet available, but the statutory target is high-risk.
AB748 — Single-Family and Multifamily Housing: Preapproved Plans
Harabedian
Mandates that cities establish a preapproved plan program for housing, creating a ministerial-style approval pathway that removes discretionary review and imposes unfunded administrative burdens on local planning departments.
SB908 — Residential Windows: Retrofitting
Wiener
Adds Government Code §65850.73 to preempt local building authority over window replacements meeting California Building Code standards, limiting cities' ability to apply locally-adopted requirements.
SB1014 — Development Projects: Preliminary Estimate of Improvements
Grayson
Requires cities to provide early binding-style estimates of required improvements to applicants, creating applicant-reliance risk that could constrain cities from imposing additional infrastructure conditions later in the review process.
 
Before You Close This

Six days is not enough time to react.Before March 7, contact your city's state legislative advocate and confirm they have AB1710 and AB1751 flagged for immediate committee tracking. Ask your planning director to inventory which spot bills on the eligibility calendar — including AB2127 and AB2079 — have not yet received substantive language, and set a text-drop alert for both. This isn't about stopping housing — it's about who decides. Every bill on this list answers that question the same way: Sacramento decides, your community absorbs the cost.

 

Watch the blank bills.The most dangerous moment in the California legislative session is not a floor vote — it is the week a spot bill gets its language and no one outside Sacramento is paying attention. The next 14 days are that window. Your job is to be ready before the hearing is noticed, not after it passes.

Local Control Intelligence
WEEK OF JUNE 29, 2026
 

Sacramento didn't move a single bill this week — and that's exactly when you should be paying closest attention. Eight bills cross committee eligibility thresholds in the next 14 days, and two of them are carrying placeholder language that could be replaced with full preemption text before a hearing is ever noticed. A quiet week is not a safe week. It's the setup.

Blank shells. Real deadlines.Content drops without warning.your window to respond.Nothing moved on the floor this week. No amendments were filed. By the standard measures of legislative momentum, Sacramento was quiet. But the action calendar tells a different story: eight bills cross their committee hearing eligibility thresholds within the next 14 days, and two of them — AB2127 on accessory dwelling units and AB2079 on adaptive reuse — are currently carrying empty spot-bill summaries. That means substantive preemption language can be inserted into either bill at any moment, a hearing can be noticed within days of that insertion, and your city's legal and policy staff may have 72 hours to respond to provisions they've never seen. The deadline isn't when the hearing is scheduled. It's right now.

HAA. Subdivisions. By-right.Three mechanisms, coordinated pressure.discretionary review, erased in sequence.The bills approaching eligibility this session don't operate in isolation — they work as a system. AB1710, eligible for its first committee hearing in six days, amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new Government Code section that further removes cities' authority to deny or condition qualifying housing projects, stripping discretionary review at the project level. AB1751, eligible March 12, creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, removing discretionary review entirely and eliminating local authority over design, density, and community input. SB677 operates on the subdivision side, amending the Subdivision Map Act and Housing Accountability Act to curtail local appeal rights on tentative and final maps while constraining the infrastructure improvement conditions cities can attach to subdivision approvals. Each bill targets a different chokepoint in the entitlement process. Together, they close off nearly every remaining avenue for meaningful local control.

No fee authority.Infrastructure demand doesn't pause.existing residents absorb the cost.The fiscal dimension of this session's preemption bills is not incidental — it is structural. AB1751 explicitly removes cities' authority to charge water, sewer, traffic, or school impact fees for new townhome residents, which means the capital costs of serving those units fall on existing ratepayers and taxpayers with no mechanism for recovery. AB2576 and AB2074 impose similar constraints in the transit-oriented development context, where ministerial approvals limit cities' ability to condition projects on utility upgrades or traffic improvements. SB1014 adds an administrative layer by requiring cities to generate preliminary infrastructure cost estimates for developers early in the entitlement process — an unfunded obligation that consumes staff capacity without giving cities any additional authority to act on what those estimates reveal. The pattern is consistent: state mandates drive development volume, state law removes the fee tools cities use to fund the infrastructure that development requires, and infrastructure cost-shifting lands on the residents who were already there.

Spot bills are the tell.Watch the shells, not the floor.how preemption actually arrives.The gut-and-amend maneuver is not a loophole — it is a legislative strategy, and this session's spot bill inventory is unusually large. SB1016, AB1899, AB1924, SB1090, and AB1795 are all currently carrying boilerplate summaries with no substantive content, parked in holding patterns that preserve their eligibility windows while authors finalize the language they plan to insert. Any one of them could become a significant preemption vehicle before the next edition of this newsletter reaches your inbox. The California Stewardship Fund's position has never been that housing shouldn't get built — it's that the people closest to the decision should make it. When Sacramento uses blank bills and compressed notice windows to remove discretionary review, mandate development patterns, and shift infrastructure costs to existing communities, it isn't accelerating housing. It's replacing accountability with speed. That distinction is worth defending, and the next 14 days are when the defense begins.

 
What to Watch
AB1710 — Housing Developments: Ordinances, Policies, and Standards
Carrillo
Amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new by-right approval pathway that preempts local authority to deny or condition qualifying housing projects, eliminating discretionary review and exposing cities to attorney-fee liability for any pushback.
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial approval pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, removes all discretionary review, and prohibits cities from charging impact fees — making infrastructure cost-shifting to existing taxpayers mandatory by statute.
SB677 — Land Use: Housing Approvals and Subdivision Maps
Wiener
Amends the Housing Accountability Act and Subdivision Map Act to curtail local appeal rights and restrict the infrastructure improvement conditions cities can attach to tentative and final map approvals.
AB2390 — Streamlined Housing Approvals: Objective Standards
Schiavo
Narrows the conditions cities may impose during SB 35 ministerial review and expands the by-right approval pathway, further removing local authority to modify or deny qualifying streamlined projects.
AB2576 — Transit-Oriented Development
Harabedian
Expands the TOD ministerial approval statute and tightens restrictions on the infrastructure conditions and impact fees cities may impose on qualifying transit-adjacent housing projects.
AB2074 — Regional Transit Hub Districts: Downtown Housing
Haney
Creates a by-right approval mandate for housing in state-defined transit hub districts, preempts local zoning authority, eliminates CEQA and design review, and limits cities' ability to require infrastructure improvements from qualifying projects.
AB748 — Single-Family and Multifamily Housing: Preapproved Plans
Harabedian
Requires cities to accept state-preapproved building plans on a ministerial basis, removing discretionary design review and compressing public input timelines across both single-family and multifamily residential projects.
AB2433 — Housing Development: Density Bonus
Alvarez
Amends the Density Bonus Law to expand qualifying projects and limit the objective standards cities may apply, extending a state preemption framework that already overrides local zoning maximums and approval conditions.
SB908 — Residential Window Replacement: Building Code Compliance
Wiener
Preempts local ordinances and HOA rules that impose standards beyond California Building Code minimums for residential window replacements, limiting cities' and associations' authority over neighborhood design and historic preservation guidelines.
SB1014 — Development Projects: Preliminary Estimate of Required Improvements
Grayson
Requires cities to provide developers with early infrastructure cost estimates — an unfunded administrative state mandate that consumes staff capacity without granting cities any additional authority to act on the findings.
 
Before You Close This

Six days. Get on the record now.AB1710 becomes eligible for its first committee hearing on March 7. Contact your Assembly member this week and ask them directly where they stand on expanding Housing Accountability Act preemption — specifically whether they support removing cities' remaining discretionary authority over project conditions and density. This isn't about stopping housing — it's about who decides. Your city council was elected to make those decisions. Sacramento is legislating that authority away, bill by bill, and the window to be heard closes before the hearing is even noticed.

 

The quiet weeks matter most.Preemption doesn't arrive with fanfare. It arrives in spot bills, eligibility windows, and compressed notice periods during weeks when nothing appears to be happening. The cities that stay ready during the quiet weeks are the ones with standing to fight when the hearings are noticed. That's where this organization operates, and that's where this fight is won.

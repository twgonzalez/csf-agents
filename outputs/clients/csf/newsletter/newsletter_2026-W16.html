WEEK OF APRIL 13, 2026
 

No votes were cast in Sacramento this week. No amendments were filed. That's not a reprieve — it's the eye of the storm. Eight bills reach committee-hearing eligibility within the next 14 days, two of them carrying empty placeholder language that could be swapped for full preemption text before anyone outside the Capitol notices. The quiet week is exactly the moment to understand how this machinery works before it moves.

Nothing moved.Everything is loading.fourteen days, eight bills.The legislature produced no floor votes, no committee hearings, and no author amendments this week — and that silence is deceptive. What the calendar actually shows is a compression zone: eight bills become eligible for their first committee hearings within the next 14 days, with AB1710 crossing that threshold in just six days on March 7, followed by AB1751 and AB1771 on March 12. Eligibility doesn't mean a hearing is scheduled — it means one can be noticed at any moment, often with as little as 72 hours' public warning. For local officials, this is the last window to get on record before Sacramento starts voting.

Spot bills. Empty shells.Preemption language incoming.your city can't see it yet.Two bills with upcoming hearing-eligibility dates — AB2127 on accessory dwelling units and AB2079 on adaptive reuse — currently carry nothing but boilerplate printer-stage summaries, meaning their substantive content hasn't been written yet. Five additional spot bills tracked this session share the same structure: SB1016, SB1090, AB1899, AB1924, and AB1795 all hold placeholder titles with single-line summaries and zero policy text. The gut-and-amend procedure allows any of these shells to receive full legislative language — including preemption of local zoning authority, removal of discretionary review, and infrastructure cost-shifting — the moment an author decides the time is right, often days before a committee hearing is noticed. By the time your planning director reads the bill, the hearing date is already set.

Transit hubs. Hazardous sites.Ministerial approval either way.the cost lands on existing taxpayers.This session's most aggressive preemption bills share a structural playbook worth understanding on its own terms. AB2074 creates a new state mandate under Government Code §65913.13, establishing a ministerial approval pathway for downtown housing in regional transit hub districts — boundaries your city didn't draw and cannot opt out of. AB2390 extends SB 35's existing by-right approval statute to housing projects on hazardous waste sites, stripping cities of the discretionary review they currently use to verify remediation before residents move in. SB677 amends the transit-oriented development streamlining statute to expand mandatory ministerial approvals near transit. In each case, the infrastructure cost-shifting mechanism is identical: qualifying projects are approved without the conditions cities would otherwise use to require developer-funded improvements, leaving water, sewer, and traffic capacity costs to be absorbed by the existing tax base.

Session pattern, now visible.Local authority, bracketed.who decides is the question.Step back from the individual bill numbers and the session strategy becomes legible. Sacramento is bracketing local authority from multiple directions simultaneously: AB1751 preempts local authority over residential parcels statewide and eliminates impact fees, AB1710 amends the Housing Accountability Act to further restrict cities' ability to apply local standards, AB2433 expands the Density Bonus Law to narrow the conditions cities may impose on qualifying projects, and AB748 mandates that cities build and administer a preapproved plan program at their own expense. None of these bills is an outlier — each one removes a specific layer of discretionary review or infrastructure protection that local governments currently rely on. The fight is not about whether California builds housing. It is about whether the people closest to the consequences of that growth retain any authority over how, where, and at whose cost it happens.

 
What to Watch
AB1710 — Housing Accountability Act Expansion
Carrillo
Amends §65589.5 and adds §65958 to further restrict cities' ability to deny or condition housing projects, expanding ministerial approval mandates and increasing HAA liability exposure for any city that applies local planning standards.
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, eliminates all discretionary review and CEQA applicability, and strips cities of impact fee authority — shifting infrastructure costs entirely to existing taxpayers.
AB2074 — Regional Transit Hub District Housing
Haney
Adds §65913.13 to create a state-mandated ministerial approval pathway for downtown housing in transit hub districts cities cannot opt out of, preempting local zoning and eliminating design review with no infrastructure cost recovery mechanism.
AB2390 — Streamlined Housing Approvals: Hazardous Waste Sites
Schiavo
Expands SB 35's by-right ministerial approval statute to housing on contaminated sites, removing the discretionary review cities use to verify adequate cleanup before residents occupy new units.
AB2433 — Density Bonus Law Expansion
Alvarez
Amends §65915 to further preempt local zoning by broadening the class of projects that qualify for mandatory density bonuses and narrowing the conditions cities may impose, compounding existing infrastructure cost-shifting without restoring any local mitigation authority.
SB677 — Transit-Oriented Development Streamlining
Wiener
Amends §65912.156 to expand mandatory ministerial approval of housing near transit, overriding local zoning and eliminating public hearings while constraining the infrastructure conditions cities may require from developers.
AB2390 — Hazardous Site Housing Streamlining
Schiavo
Extends SB 35 ministerial approvals to hazardous waste sites, removing the last local checkpoint for public health review before occupancy and preventing cities from conditioning approvals on verified remediation.
SB908 — Residential Window Retrofit Preemption
Wiener
Adds §65850.73 to mandate ministerial approval of energy-code-compliant window retrofits, stripping cities of design review and historic compatibility conditions while preempting HOA rules through a parallel Civil Code addition.
AB748 — Preapproved Housing Plans Mandate
Harabedian
Forces cities to establish and administer a preapproved plan program for single-family and multifamily housing, removing discretionary review for qualifying projects and imposing unfunded administrative costs with no state reimbursement mechanism.
SB1014 — Development Improvement Estimates Mandate
Grayson
Requires cities to produce preliminary estimates of required onsite and offsite improvements for every development applicant, creating an unfunded staffing obligation with no state reimbursement.

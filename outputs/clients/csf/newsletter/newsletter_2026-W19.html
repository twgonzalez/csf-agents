Local Control Intelligence
WEEK OF MAY 4, 2026
 

Sacramento didn't move a single bill this week — and that's exactly when you need to be paying attention. Eight bills cross their committee hearing eligibility threshold in the next 14 days, and two of them are still blank. The quiet weeks are when the gut-and-amend moves get staged.

Nothing moved this week.That's the tell.the setup happens in silence.No bills advanced a legislative stage. No amendments were filed. By the surface metrics, it was an uneventful week in Sacramento — and that reading is exactly wrong. What the calendar shows instead is a cluster of eight bills crossing their committee hearing eligibility windows within the next 14 days, with AB1710 eligible for its first committee hearing in six days and AB1751 following five days after. The quiet isn't a pause. It's the interval between the trigger being cocked and the shot.

Spot bills. Empty shells.Loaded before the notice.preemption language drops without warning.AB2127 and AB2079 are both approaching their hearing eligibility dates carrying empty spot-bill summaries — placeholder text that can be replaced with substantive preemption language at any point before a hearing is noticed. This is a known legislative maneuver, and it's how significant local control overrides avoid extended public scrutiny. AB2390, which extends SB 35 ministerial approval to housing on hazardous waste sites, and AB2601, which tightens ministerial pathways across five existing Government Code sections simultaneously, show exactly where that language tends to land: in statutes that already preempts local authority, making the expansion look incremental when it isn't. A spot bill dropped into the ADU or adaptive reuse space could follow the same arc in a matter of days.

Design review is gone.Now the bill comes due.your existing ratepayers pay it.The infrastructure cost-shifting angle is the thread connecting this entire cluster, and it runs deepest in AB1751. The Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act doesn't just remove discretionary review — it explicitly bars cities from charging water, sewer, traffic, or school impact fees for qualifying townhome development. Every unit built under that state mandate adds demand to systems that existing residents already paid to build, with zero mechanism for the city to recover those costs from the project. AB2074's regional transit hub district overlay operates the same way: ministerial approval prevents cities from conditioning projects on utility or frontage improvements, and there is no state funding backstop. SB677 extends that same infrastructure cost-shifting logic into the transit-oriented development streamlining statute. The fee ban isn't a side effect — it is the mechanism.

One session. One logic.Replicated across every statute.who decides is the only question.AB1710, AB2601, AB2074, SB677, AB2390 — these bills don't share an author or a committee, but they share an architecture: identify a local approval process that allows discretionary judgment, then mandate ministerial by-right approval instead, then bar the infrastructure conditions that cities would have attached through that process. AB2433 is doing the same work inside the Density Bonus Law. AB748 does it by forcing cities to build and administer a preapproved plan program with no guaranteed state funding. The session pattern is not a series of individual housing bills. It is a systematic campaign to remove discretionary review from every stage of the development process — entitlement, permitting, design, fees — leaving local governments with the obligation to serve new residents and none of the tools to plan for them. That's the fight. This newsletter exists because someone in your city needs to be watching it.

 
What to Watch
AB1710 — Housing Developments: Ordinances, Policies, and Standards
Carrillo
Amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new statute that preempts local authority to apply ordinances or standards that would reduce density, stripping discretionary review from housing approvals across the board.
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel, removes all discretionary review, and explicitly bars cities from collecting impact fees — shifting infrastructure costs entirely to existing taxpayers.
AB2390 — Streamlined Housing Approvals: Hazardous Waste Sites
Schiavo
Extends SB 35 ministerial approval to housing on contaminated sites, eliminating the local discretionary review that is the only remaining checkpoint for verifying remediation before residents move in.
AB2601 — Planning and Zoning: Streamlined Approval and Subdivisions
Lee
Amends five Government Code sections simultaneously to tighten and expand ministerial approval mandates, further restricting the infrastructure conditions and objective standards cities may apply to qualifying projects.
AB2074 — Regional Transit Hub Districts: Downtown Housing Developments
Haney
Creates a new state-mandated overlay that forces ministerial, by-right approval for downtown housing near transit hubs, explicitly preempting local zoning and barring infrastructure conditions cities would otherwise require.
SB677 — Housing Development: Transit-Oriented Development
Wiener
Expands California's TOD streamlining statute to mandate ministerial approval for a broader set of projects near transit, removing CEQA review, public hearings, and cities' ability to impose infrastructure conditions.
AB2433 — Housing Development: Density Bonus
Alvarez
Expands State Density Bonus Law to further override local zoning and reduce the objective standards cities may apply to qualifying projects, deepening preemption of local discretionary authority.
AB748 — Single-Family and Multifamily Housing Units: Preapproved Plans
Harabedian
Forces cities to build and administer a preapproved housing plan program that bypasses local design review and discretionary standards, with no guaranteed state funding to cover the compliance burden.
SB1014 — Development Projects: Preliminary Estimate of Required Improvements
Grayson
Requires cities to produce on-demand preliminary improvement cost estimates for applicants early in the review process — an unfunded state mandate that adds administrative burden without any cost recovery mechanism.
 
Before You Close This

Six days. Contact Housing and Community Development Committee.AB1710 becomes eligible for its first committee hearing on March 7. Contact the Assembly Housing and Community Development Committee members now and ask them directly where they stand on a bill that preempts local authority to apply the standards your city adopted through its own planning process. This isn't about stopping housing — it's about who decides. Your planning director can document the specific infrastructure and service capacity consequences for your jurisdiction. That record matters when the hearing is noticed.

 

Watch the calendar, not the votes.The weeks without floor action are when the architecture gets set — eligibility windows open, spot bills get loaded, and hearings get noticed before most councils have had a chance to respond. The fight for local control this session is being decided in the interval between introduction and first hearing. That's exactly the interval we're in right now.

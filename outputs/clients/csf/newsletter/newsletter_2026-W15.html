Local Control Intelligence
WEEK OF APRIL 6, 2026
 

The legislature didn't move bills forward this week — but it doesn't need to. Eight measures cross their hearing-eligibility threshold inside the next two weeks, and two of them are empty vessels waiting for preemption language to be dropped in before anyone notices. A quiet calendar is not a safe one. This is the week to read the procedure, not the headlines.

Nothing passed this week.Eight deadlines are coming.the window closes fast.No bills advanced a legislative stage this week, and no author amendments were filed — but the absence of floor action is not the story. AB1710, which amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new Government Code section that would further restrict cities' ability to deny or condition housing projects, becomes eligible for its first committee hearing in six days, on March 7. AB1751 and AB1771 follow five days later on March 12. Eight bills in total cross their hearing-eligibility thresholds within the next 14 days. The calendar looks quiet. The mechanics are not.

Blank bills. Real deadlines.Language drops without warning.your city has no notice.AB2127 and AB2079 are currently carrying empty spot-bill summaries — substantive preemption language can be inserted into either at any moment before their hearings are noticed. That is not a hypothetical; it is standard Sacramento procedure, and it is specifically how gut-and-amend works at the committee stage. Meanwhile, AB2390 sits in the same eligibility window and would extend California's SB 35 ministerial approval statute to housing projects on hazardous waste sites, stripping cities of the discretionary review authority they currently use to verify adequate remediation before residents move in. Cities that aren't tracking spot bills alongside the named ones are watching half the board.

Design review is gone.So is the cleanup checkpoint.liability lands on your taxpayers.The fiscal angle on this session's bills is not limited to impact fees. AB2390 removes local authority to condition or delay approvals pending independent verification of site cleanup — meaning cities can be forced to permit residential development on contaminated land with no mechanism to hold developers accountable for remediation. SB1014, authored by Grayson, directly targets the infrastructure cost-shifting problem from another direction: by adding new Government Code sections that appear to restrict or standardize the nexus and rough-proportionality conditions cities impose on developers for street dedications, utility extensions, and frontage upgrades. Constrain improvement conditions and you have shifted capacity costs to existing residents without a vote, a hearing, or a line item in the city budget.

Transit hubs. Hazardous sites. Lot splits.Same mechanism, different addresses.the pattern is the point.AB2074 creates a ministerial by-right approval pathway for downtown housing developments in regional transit hub districts, explicitly preempting local zoning and removing CEQA discretion — while its appropriation language limits cities' ability to require developer-funded infrastructure improvements commensurate with project impacts. AB2005 amends SB 9's urban lot split statute, historically a vector for expanding the universe of parcels subject to ministerial approval. AB2390 takes the same ministerial framework to contaminated land. These are not independent bills pursuing independent goals; they are the same override architecture applied to a new address each session. The question your council should be asking right now is not which bill is worst — it is how many addresses Sacramento has left to cover.

 
What to Watch
AB1710 — Housing Developments: Ordinances, Policies, and Standards
Carrillo
Amends the Housing Accountability Act and adds a new preemption section that further strips cities of authority to deny or condition housing projects — eligible for committee in six days.
AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act
Quirk-Silva
Creates a statewide ministerial pathway for townhomes on any residential parcel and removes local authority to charge infrastructure impact fees, shifting all capacity costs to existing taxpayers.
AB2390 — Streamlined Housing Approvals: Hazardous Waste Sites
Schiavo
Extends SB 35's no-discretion ministerial approval pathway to housing on contaminated sites, eliminating the local review authority cities use to verify remediation before occupancy.
AB2074 — Regional Transit Hub Districts: Downtown Housing Developments
Haney
Creates a state mandate for ministerial by-right approval of downtown transit-adjacent housing, explicitly preempting local zoning and removing all discretionary review including CEQA.
AB2005 — Housing Developments: Urban Lot Split: Parcel Maps
Ahrens
Amends SB 9's urban lot split statute in a direction that historically expands ministerial approval scope, removing city discretion over subdivision and infrastructure conditioning.
SB1014 — Development Projects: Onsite and Offsite Improvements
Grayson
Targets the infrastructure improvement conditions cities impose on developers, restricting nexus and rough-proportionality requirements and shifting capacity costs onto existing residents.
AB2601 — Planning and Zoning: Housing Development: Streamlined Approval and Subdivisions
Lee
Amends five existing streamlined-approval statutes simultaneously, each of which already removes local discretionary review — amendments in this cluster almost always expand preemption scope.
SB677 — Housing Development: Transit-Oriented Development
Wiener
Amends the TOD streamlining statute to expand or strengthen mandatory ministerial approval near transit, overriding local zoning and removing the infrastructure conditions cities depend on.
AB2433 — Housing Development: Density Bonus
Alvarez
Amends the Density Bonus Law to expand state mandates compelling cities to grant density increases and concessions beyond what local zoning would permit — monitor for eligibility and concession expansions.
AB748 — Single-Family and Multifamily Housing Units: Preapproved Plans
Harabedian
Forces cities to establish a preapproved housing plan program that bypasses local design review and imposes unfunded administrative infrastructure on planning departments with no state offset.
 
Before You Close This

Six days. Brief your council now.Before March 7, send your council members and planning director the AB1710 bill text alongside this watch list. Ask them directly: does your city have a formal comment letter process for Housing Accountability Act amendments? If not, this is the week to build one. This isn't about stopping housing — it's about who decides. The answer to that question gets written in committee, not at the general plan hearing.

 

The calendar rewards preparation.A quiet legislative week is the right moment to close the gaps — track the spot bills, read the full text of AB1710 before it's noticed, and make sure your planning director isn't watching the named bills while the blank ones fill in. The session pattern is established. The specific addresses change. The local control infrastructure you build this month is the only thing standing between your community and a vote that was never held.

AB1751, the Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act (Quirk-Silva), introduces one of the most sweeping zoning preemptions of the current legislative session.

The bill adds Section 65852.29 to the Government Code, establishing a statewide ministerial approval pathway for townhome development on any residential parcel. Once a project meets state objective standards, cities must approve it — regardless of neighborhood infrastructure capacity, existing utility load, or local planning priorities. No discretionary review applies. No CEQA review applies. No public hearings are required.

The fiscal mechanism is equally significant: the bill eliminates city authority to charge water, sewer, traffic, or school impact fees for qualifying townhome developments. Infrastructure costs generated by new residents do not stay with the developer — they transfer to existing taxpayers and the municipal general fund.

This is not a targeted infill measure. It applies to any residential parcel statewide, and it sets a statutory precedent for further ministerial preemption of residential zoning in future sessions.

City attorneys, planning directors, and public works officials should review AB1751 now and document infrastructure capacity constraints in their jurisdictions. Formal comment letters to the Assembly Housing Committee are the appropriate next step. CPRC's full bill analysis is available at the link below.

No hearings are scheduled this week, but AB2288 (Quirk-Silva) is moving and warrants immediate attention from city officials and economic development professionals.

The bill amends Sections 65658.1 and 65658.5 of the Government Code to require cities to permit residential conversions in industrially zoned areas — directly preempting employment land designations that communities have established through years of local planning.

The infrastructure problem is concrete: industrial zones were not designed for residential occupancy. They typically lack the water capacity, sewer connections, pedestrian infrastructure, and park access that residential use requires. AB2288 bars cities from requiring developers to fund those upgrades as a condition of conversion approval. Those costs fall instead to the municipality.

The economic development stakes are equally significant. Industrial land converted to residential cannot be converted back. Cities that rely on employment zones for tax-generating commercial and industrial activity face a permanent, one-way land use change with no fiscal offset.

We are asking planning directors, city managers, and economic development officers to contact their Assembly member this week with documented evidence of employment land and infrastructure constraints in their jurisdiction. CPRC's bill analysis is available via the link in our profile.

AB1751, AB2288, and SB1014 share one mechanism: removing the conditions cities attach to development approvals. Preemption of fees, zoning, and improvement requirements. The fiscal burden lands on existing residents. #HousingPolicy

This week's legislative activity reflects a consistent structural pattern that merits examination beyond any single bill.

AB1751 eliminates impact fee authority for townhome development. AB2288 bars cities from conditioning industrial-to-residential conversions on developer-funded infrastructure upgrades. SB1014 restricts cities' ability to require offsite improvements as conditions of housing approvals. Each bill operates on a distinct statutory mechanism — but the downstream effect is identical in each case: the cost of growth-related infrastructure moves from the developer to the existing taxpayer.

This is not an accident of legislative drafting. It reflects a deliberate policy choice to accelerate housing production by reducing the cost basis of development. That tradeoff may be defensible on production grounds. What it does not do is resolve the underlying question of who funds the water lines, traffic signals, and sewer capacity that new residents require.

The Supreme Court's recent denial of Huntington Beach's housing mandate appeal confirms that federal judicial review is not a viable check on state preemption. The avenue for local governments is legislative: documented, evidence-based analysis submitted early in the committee process, before bills move to floor votes.

CPRC produces that analysis. Our role is to ensure that policymakers, journalists, and city officials have precise, nonpartisan evidence about what each mechanism does — before the vote, not after. Follow our work and share it with the people making these decisions.

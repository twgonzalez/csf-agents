AB1751 — Missing Middle Townhome Ownership Act: What City Managers Need to Know Now

AB1751, introduced by Assemblymember Sharon Quirk-Silva, creates a statewide ministerial approval pathway for townhome development on any residential parcel in California. For city managers, the operational implications are immediate and significant.

Under this bill, any townhome project meeting state objective standards must be approved — regardless of your city's infrastructure capacity, local planning priorities, or neighborhood context. Discretionary review is eliminated. Design review and public hearings do not apply. CEQA review is waived.

Perhaps most consequentially for municipal budgets: cities lose all impact fee authority for qualifying projects. Water, sewer, traffic, and school impact fees cannot be charged to new townhome residents. The infrastructure cost of serving those residents shifts entirely to your existing taxpayers and general fund.

This is not a theoretical risk. It is a direct preemption of fee authority that cities rely on to fund the capital improvements that growth requires.

We recommend members coordinate immediately with planning directors and city attorneys to document current infrastructure capacity constraints and prepare fiscal impact analyses for your council. Formal comment letters opposing AB1751 should be submitted as the bill advances through committee.

Action Alert: AB2288 Threatens Employment Land and Municipal Infrastructure Funding

AB2288, also introduced by Assemblymember Sharon Quirk-Silva, mandates that cities allow residential conversions in industrially zoned areas — directly preempting the employment land protections communities have built into their general plans over decades.

The operational problems are layered. Industrial zones are not designed for residential occupancy. They frequently lack the water, sewer, pedestrian infrastructure, and park access that residential use requires. Under AB2288, cities cannot require developers to fund those upgrades. That cost falls to your general fund and existing ratepayers.

Beyond infrastructure, this bill creates a one-way transition. Employment land converted to residential cannot be recovered. Cities lose the tax-generating industrial and commercial uses that anchor economic development strategies — and the jobs that depend on them.

The bill also removes local environmental review, meaning conversions proceed ministerially even on sites with contamination histories or incompatible adjacent industrial operations.

We recommend members coordinate with economic development officers, public works directors, and city attorneys to document employment land inventories and infrastructure gap analyses. Formal opposition letters should be prepared in partnership with economic development and labor organizations. This bill has real consequences for long-term fiscal health — council needs a briefing now.

The Pattern Behind This Week's Bills

This week's legislative activity — AB1751, AB2288, and SB1014 — reflects a pattern that California city managers know well: state law determines the land use outcome, and local government absorbs the implementation cost.

AB1751 mandates ministerial townhome approvals and eliminates impact fee authority. AB2288 forces residential conversion of industrial land without any developer obligation to fund the infrastructure residential occupancy requires. SB1014 restricts cities' ability to require offsite improvements from developers, shifting infrastructure costs to existing residents and the general fund.

The Supreme Court's recent denial of Huntington Beach's appeal reinforces what our members already understand: the legal terrain for local control challenges is narrow. The professional response is not litigation — it is documentation, fiscal analysis, and early engagement in the legislative process.

Cities across California support housing production. What our members are managing is the cumulative operational consequence of legislation that assigns cities the responsibility for outcomes without the resources or authority to achieve them responsibly. Every impact fee preempted is a capital project deferred. Every compressed timeline is a technical review that cannot be completed. Every employment land conversion is an economic strategy unraveling.

CMA will continue tracking these bills and coordinating member response. The profession's credibility depends on clear, factual engagement — and that work starts at the city manager's desk.
